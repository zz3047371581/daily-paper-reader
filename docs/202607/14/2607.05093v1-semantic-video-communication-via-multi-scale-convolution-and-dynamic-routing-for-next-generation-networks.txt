Abstract —The exponential growth of video traffic demands novel semantic communication paradigms that transmit meaning rather than raw bits. We present a generative AI-enabled framework for semantic video communication addressing two critical challenges: efficient hierarchical temporal modeling for bandwidth-constrained transmission and robust semantic align-ment between video content and natural language queries at network edge devices. Our approach introduces a multi-scale temporal convolutional encoder that captures motion patterns across different temporal granularities with O(T) complexity suitable for resource-constrained IoT deployments. We further propose a capsule-based dynamic routing mechanism that it-eratively refines segment–query associations, enabling flexible modeling of non-monotonic semantic alignments essential for goal-oriented communication. These components are unified through a multi-task learning objective optimizing temporal boundary regression, cross-modal alignment, and capsule diver-sity. Experiments on ActivityNet Captions demonstrate significant improvements, achieving 42.9% Recall@0.5 and 41.1% mean IoU while maintaining computational efficiency critical for edge deployment. 

Index Terms —Semantic communication, generative AI, video understanding, next-generation networks, edge computing, goal-oriented communication 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

The proliferation of video content has created unprece-dented challenges for next-generation communication net-works. Video traffic now accounts for over 80% of global internet bandwidth, placing enormous strain on network infras-tructure and demanding innovative solutions beyond traditional Shannon-paradigm communication that focuses on accurate bit transmission [11], [14], [17], [31]. Semantic communication, which aims to transmit the meaning of information rather than raw data, has emerged as a promising paradigm for addressing this challenge, with applications spanning content-based video retrieval, human–robot interaction over wireless networks, and intelligent video streaming for IoT devices [5], [20]. At the core of semantic video communication lies the funda-mental problem of video–text temporal localization—precisely aligning natural language semantic queries with corresponding video segments. This capability enables revolutionary network applications: instead of transmitting entire video streams, edge devices can process semantic queries locally and transmit only relevant segments, dramatically reducing bandwidth consump-tion. Furthermore, goal-oriented communication protocols can leverage such alignment to ensure Quality of Experience (QoE) by prioritizing semantically important content during network congestion. Despite recent advances in generative AI and vi-sion–language modeling, achieving fine-grained temporal se-mantic alignment remains fundamentally challenging for net-work deployment. Current pretrained models such as CLIP [24], BLIP [19], and their video-adapted variants (e.g., CLIP4Clip [22], VideoBERT [25], X-CLIP [23]) excel at coarse-grained video–text matching but suffer from two criti-cal limitations for network applications: (1) prohibitive com-putational complexity unsuitable for edge devices and IoT sensors, and (2) lack of temporal resolution necessary for precise semantic boundary identification [21]. The challenge is particularly acute in bandwidth-constrained scenarios where multiple related semantic concepts occur in close temporal proximity with ambiguous boundaries. Con-sider a surveillance video being transmitted over a low-bandwidth IoT network where queries like ”person entering the building” and ”person walking through the lobby” may correspond to overlapping temporal regions. As illustrated in Figure 1, real-world semantic communication scenarios frequently exhibit three types of complexity: (1) single se-mantic queries spanning multiple disjoint video segments, (2) multiple queries mapping to overlapping temporal regions, and (3) subtle transitions between semantically related actions. Existing methods, which predominantly rely on computa-tionally expensive global attention mechanisms or assume monotonic alignments, fail to meet the efficiency and accuracy requirements of next-generation network applications. We identify two fundamental limitations in current ap-proaches that hinder their deployment in next-generation net-works. First, most methods lack explicit hierarchical tem-

This holistic approach ensures that the model learns both precise temporal localization and robust semantic matching, addressing the full spectrum of challenges in semantic video communication for next-generation networks.

A. Semantic Communication and Video–Text Alignment 

Semantic communication has emerged as a transformative paradigm for next-generation networks, shifting focus from bit-level accuracy to meaning-level transmission. The task of temporally localizing video segments from natural language semantic queries is fundamental to this paradigm and has evolved through several approaches. Early proposal-based methods generate candidate segments and refine them through regression. TALL [11] pioneered this approach by combining temporal proposal generation with multimodal feature fusion, while ABLR [29] enhanced alignment through attention-based boundary refinement and semantic conditioning. The field shifted toward end-to-end span prediction meth-ods that directly regress temporal boundaries. VSLNet [32] reformulated the problem as span-based question answering, introducing query-guided highlighting for feature enhance-ment. 2D-TAN [33] and DRN [30] advanced this direction by constructing 2D temporal maps that model all possible segment spans simultaneously, enabling more comprehensive temporal reasoning. While these supervised methods achieve strong performance on benchmarks like ActivityNet Captions [2], they require expensive frame-level annotations and exhibit O(T²) complexity unsuitable for network edge deployment. Weakly supervised approaches emerged to address annota-tion costs and computational constraints. WS-DEC [6] learns from video–sentence pairs without temporal annotations us-ing discriminative clustering, while TGA [28] employs tree-structured attention for hierarchical alignment. However, these methods often assume monotonic or coarse segment-level correspondence, limiting their applicability to complex seman-tic communication scenarios with overlapping or disordered events. Recent innovations include metric learning frameworks [33], reinforcement learning for boundary refinement, and graph-based reasoning [8] for modeling inter-segment relation-ships. The latest generation leverages large-scale pretraining and language model capabilities critical for generative AI-native network architectures. Video-Text Prompting (VTP) [34] in-serts learnable prompts into both modalities to bridge the pretraining-finetuning gap under weak supervision. TFVTG [35] decomposes queries into sub-events using large language models for training-free zero-shot grounding [12]. Pretrained video–language models like HERO [20] and VIOLET [10] employ masked modeling and contrastive objectives on large-scale corpora, though their sentence-level supervision limits temporal precision required for goal-oriented communication. LocVTP [3] addresses this through localization-aware pretrain-ing, but still operates at relatively coarse temporal resolution. For finer-grained alignment essential for semantic commu-nication protocols, token-level strategies have shown promise. VT-TWINS [15] introduces differentiable weak alignment between frames and words, while TANet [13] applies multi-scale temporal attention for long-form videos. Despite these advances, most methods struggle with complex many-to-many correspondences common in real-world network scenarios. 

B. Efficient GenAI Models for Network Edge Deployment 

Multi-scale temporal modeling has proven essential for video understanding while offering potential for computa-tional efficiency. Architectures like I3D [4], SlowFast [9], and TimeSformer [1] demonstrate the effectiveness of processing video at multiple temporal resolutions. In temporal ground-ing, 2D-TAN [33], DRN [30], and TANet [13] incorporate hierarchical structures through pyramidal feature extraction or multi-scale attention mechanisms. Recent work has explored how large language models can enhance video understanding for network applications. BLIVA [18] leverages LLMs for compositional reasoning across modalities, while InstructVid [7] uses instruction tuning to improve task generalization. TRACE [12] models causal sub-event structures for enhanced temporal reasoning, and recent architectures [27] introduce semantic gating to balance global context with local details. 

Our approach differs fundamentally by combining ef-ficient multi-scale convolution with capsule-based dynamic routing, specifically designed for semantic communication in next-generation networks. This design explicitly models hierarchical temporal structure with O(T) complexity suitable for edge devices while enabling flexible many-to-many seman-tic alignments through iterative refinement. Unlike methods requiring expensive end-to-end finetuning, we achieve strong performance through targeted architectural innovations and multi-task supervision, maintaining computational efficiency critical for resource-constrained network deployment. III. M ETHODOLOGY 

A. Problem Formulation and Overview 

Given an untrimmed video V containing T frames transmit-ted over a communication network and a natural language se-mantic query Q representing the receiver’s goal, our objective is to predict the temporal segment (ˆ tstart , ˆtend ) that semantically corresponds to Q. This enables goal-oriented communica-tion where only relevant video segments need transmission, dramatically reducing bandwidth requirements. This requires solving two interrelated challenges: (1) modeling temporal dynamics at multiple scales with computational efficiency suit-able for edge deployment, and (2) establishing robust semantic alignment between visual content and linguistic descriptions despite complex many-to-many correspondences inherent in semantic communication. Our framework addresses these challenges through four key components illustrated in Figure 2. First, we extract frame-level and token-level features using frozen pretrained encoders. Second, a multi-scale temporal encoder captures hierarchi-cal motion patterns through parallel convolutions with O(T) complexity. Third, a capsule-based dynamic routing module iteratively refines cross-modal semantic alignments. Finally, a temporal localization head predicts segment boundaries using features enhanced by the previous modules. These components are jointly optimized through a unified multi-task objective. 

B. Feature Extraction 

We leverage pretrained CLIP encoders [24] to extract initial representations. For video encoding, we uniformly sample frames at 1 fps and resize to 224 × 224 pixels. Each frame is independently processed through CLIP’s visual encoder (e.g., ViT-B/32), yielding a feature sequence V ∈ RT ×D, where D

is the embedding dimension. For textual encoding representing semantic queries, queries are tokenized and passed through a pretrained text encoder (CLIP Text or BERT), producing token embeddings Q ∈

RN ×D , where N is the sequence length. Both visual and textual features are projected to a shared 384-dimensional space via learnable linear transformations: 

V′ = VW v + bv (1) 

Q′ = QW q + bq (2) Since CLIP processes frames independently without tempo-ral context, we inject temporal information through sinusoidal positional embeddings [26], enabling the model to reason about temporal ordering and distances essential for precise semantic boundary identification.

Traditional ap-proaches using fixed-size temporal windows or global attention fail to capture this hierarchical structure effectively and exhibit O(T²) complexity unsuitable for network edge devices. We propose a multi-scale temporal convolutional encoder 

that processes the video representation through parallel 1D convolutions with different kernel sizes. G. Design Rationale and Efficiency Analysis 

Our framework addresses the core challenges of semantic video communication for next-generation networks through targeted architectural innovations. The multi-scale encoder provides O(T) complexity temporal modeling compared to O(T²) for attention, crucial for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices and IoT sensors. The capsule routing mechanism enables structured many-to-many semantic align-ments that attention struggles to achieve, essential for flexible goal-oriented communication protocols. Together with multi-task supervision, these components form a cohesive system that achieves strong performance without expensive end-to-end finetuning of large models, maintaining the computational efficiency required for practical network deployment. IV. E XPERIMENTS 

A. Experimental Setup 

We evaluate on ActivityNet Captions [2], [16], contain-ing 20K untrimmed YouTube videos with 100K+ temporally annotated sentence descriptions. This benchmark represents diverse real-world video content typical of network video traffic. Following standard protocol, we use 37,421 training and 17,505 validation instances. Videos are processed at 1 fps with frames resized to 224 × 224 . Segments shorter than 2 seconds or with inconsistent annotations are filtered out. Performance is measured using Recall@K at IoU thresholds 

{0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 } and mean IoU (mIoU). Higher values indicate better temporal localization accuracy, directly corre-lating with bandwidth efficiency in semantic communication scenarios where only correctly identified segments need trans-mission. 

B. Implementation Details 

Our PyTorch implementation uses frozen CLIP encoders (ViT-B/32, ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14) for visual features and var-ious text encoders (CLIP Text, BERT-base, RoBERTa-base). Features are projected to 384 dimensions. The multi-scale encoder uses kernels {1, 3, 5 } with 96-dimensional outputs. The capsule module employs 8 capsules of dimension 96 with 3 routing iterations. Training uses batch size 32, Adam optimizer with learning rate 10 −4, weight decay 10 −4, and dropout 0.15. We train for up to 50 epochs with early stopping based on validation mIoU. All experiments use a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. 

C. Main Results 1) Impact of Visual Backbone: Table I presents results with different CLIP visual encoders using the default text encoder. Larger vision models consistently improve performance, with ViT-L/14 achieving the best results (R@0.5: 0.400, mIoU: 0.391). This demonstrates that stronger visual representations enhance both semantic understanding and temporal local-ization accuracy. For edge deployment scenarios with strict resource constraints, the lighter ViT-B/32 backbone offers an excellent accuracy-efficiency trade-off. 

2) Effect of Text Encoder: Table II shows performance with different text encoders using fixed ViT-B/32 visual fea-tures. BERT-base significantly outperforms other encoders, achieving R@0.5 of 0.429 and mIoU of 0.411—a substan-tial improvement over the default CLIP text encoder. This highlights the critical role of rich textual representations in semantic video communication. Interestingly, the gains from upgrading the text encoder exceed those from scaling the visual backbone, suggesting that linguistic understanding of semantic queries is a key bottleneck in current approaches. 

3) Comparison with Baselines: While we focus on ar-chitectural innovations for network deployment rather than exhaustive comparisons, our method achieves competitive per-formance on ActivityNet Captions. The combination of multi-scale temporal modeling and capsule-based alignment provides consistent improvements over vanilla transformer baselines across all metrics, validating our design choices for semantic communication systems. 

D. Ablation Study 

Tables III and IV present comprehensive ablations exam-ining the contribution of each component. Starting from a baseline using only frozen transformers, we progressively add the multi-scale encoder and capsule alignment module. The multi-scale encoder alone improves performance mod-erately, with larger gains at lower IoU thresholds where coarse temporal understanding suffices. Importantly, it maintains O(T) complexity essential for edge deployment. The capsule module provides more substantial improvements, particularly for high-IoU metrics requiring precise alignment critical for bandwidth-efficient semantic communication. Crucially, com-bining both components yields gains exceeding their individ-ual contributions—the full model improves mIoU by 17.7% (0.328 →0.386) over the baseline with ViT-B/32. This synergy validates our hypothesis that hierarchical tem-poral modeling and structured semantic alignment are comple-mentary: multi-scale features provide rich temporal context that enhances routing decisions, while capsule alignments help the temporal encoder focus on semantically relevant scales. The consistent improvements across different back-bones demonstrate the generalizability of our approach for diverse network deployment scenarios. V. C ONCLUSION 

We presented a novel generative AI-enabled framework for semantic video communication that addresses fundamental challenges in next-generation networks through two key in-novations. First, our multi-scale temporal encoder efficiently captures hierarchical motion patterns with O(T) complexity, providing comprehensive temporal coverage from instanta-neous transitions to extended actions while enabling deploy-ment on resource-constrained edge devices and IoT sensors. Second, our capsule-based dynamic routing mechanism mod-els complex many-to-many semantic correspondences through iterative refinement, producing structured and interpretable alignments essential for goal-oriented communication proto-cols. The synergy between these components, unified through multi-task learning, achieves significant improvements on ActivityNet Captions—demonstrating 42.9% Recall@0.5 and 41.1% mIoU. Notably, our lightweight approach maintains strong performance with frozen backbone encoders, avoiding expensive end-to-end finetuning while remaining computation-ally efficient for practical network deployment in bandwidth-constrained environments. Our ablation studies reveal important insights for seman-tic communication system design: (1) hierarchical temporal modeling and structured alignment are complementary, with combined gains exceeding individual contributions; (2) text encoder quality significantly impacts performance, suggesting that understanding semantic queries is a key bottleneck; (3) soft supervision strategies better handle temporal ambiguities in real-world network video traffic. Future work will explore three directions aligned with next-generation network requirements: incorporating limited visual adaptation while preserving edge deployment efficiency, extending to cross-dataset and zero-shot scenarios for gener-alized semantic communication, and applying our framework to related network applications like adaptive video streaming and distributed video analytics. We believe our design prin-ciples—combining efficient multi-scale modeling with struc-tured semantic alignment mechanisms—offer a promising path toward more capable and deployable semantic video commu-nication systems for next-generation networks.
