Abstract 

Reasoning Language Models (RLMs) have significantly improved performance on com-plex tasks by extending the reasoning chain. However, these chains are prone to contain-ing factual errors, particularly in knowledge-intensive tasks. To address this issue, we pro-pose CheckRLM , a framework that improves the reliability of the reasoning process through Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) by timely checking and correcting factual errors. Specifically, CheckRLM extracts factual claims from the reasoning chain to identify and lo-calize subtle knowledge inconsistencies dur-ing inference. Upon detection of errors, a re-finement mechanism performs minimal-cost yet precise corrections by leveraging external knowledge, ensuring coherence between the reasoning chain and correct knowledge. Exten-sive experiments demonstrate that CheckRLM substantially outperforms existing baselines, exhibiting a strong capability to mitigate error accumulation in long-horizon reasoning with lower costs. The code and data are available at 

https://github.com/AI9Stars/CheckRLM .

By decomposing complex problems and engaging in iterative exploration and reflection over long rea-soning chains, RLMs attract significant attention in logic-intensive domains, including mathematics and coding (Chen et al., 2025; Li, 2025). To effectively check and refine reasoning chains, CheckRLM comprises two components: in-process knowledge claim recognition and localized knowl-edge coherence correction via retrieval. During in-process knowledge claim recognition, key fac-tual claims pertinent to the query are identified and extracted from each newly generated reasoning segment, facilitating the location of potential in-consistencies while minimizing interference from irrelevant information. In the localized knowledge coherence correction via retrieval process, we use the extracted key factual claims along with the orig-inal question to search an external knowledge base and perform token-level precise corrections, en-suring that each segment of the verified reasoning chain aligns with external knowledge and reducing error accumulation. In summary, our contributions are as follows: 1) we propose CheckRLM, an efficient RAG frame-work with in-reasoning knowledge checking that identifies and corrects factual errors during long-chain reasoning, effectively preventing error accu-mulation; 2) we introduce a localized intermediate intervention mechanism that dynamically extracts and verifies critical knowledge claims from partial reasoning states, enabling timely and fine-grained factual correction; 3) CheckRLM significantly out-performs recent strong baselines, while comprehen-sive analyses show that it enables early, targeted er-ror correction and simultaneously achieves top-tier performance with significantly reduced inference time and token consumption. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Reasoning Language Model (RLM) 

RLMs enhance the quality of final answers by guid-ing Large Language Models (LLMs) to decompose complex tasks into multiple subquestions and by extending step-by-step reasoning chains (Chen et al., 2025; Gao et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2026; Li, 2025). Prior work primarily relies on engineered prompts during inference without modifying the in-ternal parameters of LLMs (Yasunaga et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023). Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) in-serts several task-oriented exemplars that contain intermediate reasoning steps into the prompt, while (Kojima et al., 2022) stimulates zero-shot gener-ation of intermediate reasoning by adding the in-struction “ let’s think step by step ” to task instruc-tions, effectively eliciting reasoning capabilities in LLMs. However, these prompt-engineering ap-proaches are largely constrained by the quality of the prompts and exemplars, and the depth and cor-rectness of the elicited reasoning are difficult to guarantee. Recent studies (OpenAI, 2024; Guo et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025) have focused on deep optimization of LLMs via large-scale rein-forcement learning (RL). By designing verifiable reward functions, RLMs exhibit high-level reason-ing behaviors, including self-verification, reflec-tion, and dynamic strategy adjustment; furthermore, increasing test-time compute yields continued im-provements in reasoning performance (Weng et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2025). Meanwhile, concurrent studies have pointed out that these RLMs tend to produce hallucinations within their long reasoning chains (He et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2025). An early factual error can subsequently mislead the en-tire reasoning trajectory, leading to a phenomenon known as “error accumulation” (Ling et al., 2023; Tyen et al., 2024). 

2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) 

RAG effectively mitigates the “hallucination” in knowledge-intensive tasks by integrating LLMs with external knowledge bases, improving the accu-racy and reliability of content (Lewis et al., 2020; Ram et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2025). However, the single-retrieval mechanism in vanilla RAG (Izacard et al., 2023) often yields limited information, struggling to cover the com-plete knowledge background required for complex questions (Wang et al., 2025, 2024). To address the limitations of Vanilla RAG, researchers have proposed the adaptive RAG framework (Jeong et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023; Asai et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2022), which employs an intelligent decision-making mechanism to dynamically deter-mine when to retrieve, what to retrieve, and how to integrate the retrieved information. This sig-nificantly improves both the response quality and efficiency of RAG systems. However, these tra-ditional adaptive RAG methods are not suitable for RLMs based on long chains of thought. Con-current work (Hu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025; Guan et al., 2026; Xiong et al., 2025; Zhao et al., 2025; Li et al., 2025) combines reasoning with search, inserting external knowledge into long rea-soning chains to augment RLMs’ reasoning abil-ity. 3.1 Preliminary of CheckRLM 

Considering a complicated question q that requires multi-step reasoning, our goal is to generate a cor-rect final answer a by checking and refining the reasoning chain R generated by RLMs. Mathemat-ically, the reasoning chain R can be defined as a sequence τ = ( s1, s 2, . . . , s T ), where each st rep-resents an intermediate reasoning state generated at time step t. 3.2 Knowledge–Thought Coherence Checking 

In this section, we first introduce a two-stage Knowledge–Thought Coherence Checking process (§3.2.1 and §3.2.2), and then describe the training data construction pipeline and the training process (§3.2.3). The overall framework of CheckRLM is shown in Figure 2. 

3.2.1 In-Process Knowledge Claim Recognition 

We adopt an “ intermediate intervention ” strategy to extract explicit knowledge claims during the rea-soning process, rather than intervening only after the complete reasoning chain is generated. This strategy aims to address potential factual errors and prevent the gradual amplification of randomly oc-curring erroneous knowledge in the intermediate states of the reasoning chain at an early stage. Specifically, we identify and extract factual in-formation that supports problem-solving from the reasoning chain, summarizing it into multiple ex-plicit factual claims. These factual claims serve the fact verification model Mrec to prioritize question-relevant facts and facilitate the detection of ex-plicit errors for correction, preventing their prop-agation and negative impact on downstream rea-soning. Considering that granular checks, such as token-level or sentence-level, disrupt the logic and semantic meaning of the reasoning chain and 3trigger unnecessary or frequent interruptions, we perform coherence checks at the paragraph level, keeping a balance between contextual complete-ness and timely verification. 4.1 Datasets & Metrics Multi-hop QA Datasets (1) HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) requires retrieving and reasoning over multiple Wikipedia documents with distracting information to answer. (2) 2WikiMultiHopQA (2WikiMQA) (Ho et al., 2020) is a multi-hop QA dataset where each question requires reason-ing over entity-centric hyperlinks across multiple Wikipedia pages to discover bridging entities. (3)

IIRC (Ferguson et al., 2020) builds on Wikipedia articles where questions require identifying rele-vant text snippets from linked contexts to answer questions given an incomplete context. For the test samples and retrieval corpus, we follow IRCoT (Trivedi et al., 2023). 

Short-form QA Dataset focuses on retrieving direct answers to factoid questions from a single context. We choose SimpleQA (Wei et al., 2024), a large-scale benchmark consisting of short, fact-seeking questions. We randomly select 500 sam-ples from the test split and use the KILT (Petroni et al., 2021) knowledge source as the corpus. 

Metrics We use F1-Score (f1) and Exact Match (em) (Jiang et al., 2023) to evaluate the correctness of the final answer. 

4.2 Baselines 

We compare our method against the following base-line approaches: (1) Direct Reasoning: the RLM relies solely on its parametric knowledge to an-swer questions without retrieving any external in-formation. (2) Vanilla RAG: a single-step retrieval fetches the top-k most relevant documents, which are then provided alongside the question as context for the LLM to generate an answer. (3) Multi-Step RAG: the RAG system gathers relevant informa-tion through multiple iterative retrieval steps. We select RAT (Li et al., 2024) as our representative baseline. (4) Adaptive RAG: the RAG framework dynamically decides the retrieval and generation strategy to optimize both response quality and ef-ficiency. We primarily compare against three es-tablished baselines: FLARE (Jiang et al., 2023), Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2024), and ReAct (Yao et al., 2022). Additionally, we also compare with Search-o1 (Li et al., 2025), which actively retrieves external knowledge during the reasoning process and seamlessly integrates refined, concise informa-tion extracted from lengthy retrieval results into the reasoning chain.

5 Results and Analysis 

5.1 Overall Performance Vanilla RAG underperforms on complex tasks; by contrast, CheckRLM demonstrates a sub-stantial performance improvement. As shown in Table 1, Vanilla RAG improves upon Direct Rea-soning by introducing external knowledge docu-ments in a one-time retrieval step, thus partially al-leviating the limitation that Direct Reasoning relies entirely on internal knowledge during the reason-ing process. Empirical results indicate that Vanilla RAG achieves reasonable performance on short-form QA tasks, as these tasks typically require only a small number of relevant snippets that are most relevant to the original question; however, these ob-served performance improvements struggle to gen-eralize to complex multi-hop QA tasks. In contrast, CheckRLM consistently outperforms Vanilla RAG across all datasets. In particular, it achieves espe-cially substantial gains on complex multi-hop QA tasks, yielding improvements over Vanilla RAG across all three reasoning backbones. These perfor-mance improvements indicate that CheckRLM is capable of performing effective knowledge correc-tion by repeatedly and timely incorporating exter-nal knowledge to precisely correct factual errors in the reasoning chain.

5.2 Effectiveness of Knowledge Recognition and Correction Optimization 

The DPO training process in CheckRLM enhances the model’s ability to correct factual errors in the reasoning chain by jointly optimizing knowledge claim recognition and knowledge coherence cor-rection. Based on this design, we evaluate the per-formance gains brought by this joint optimization strategy to validate the effectiveness of our training procedure. CheckRLM introduces an intermediate interven-tion strategy that integrates knowledge claim recog-nition with consistency correction for in-reasoning check. Benchmarked against post-reasoning check, CheckRLM consistently outperforms across all datasets (Figure 3). The results confirm the su-periority of in-reasoning intervention. RLMs often introduce errors early in inference, which then propagate and amplify through subse-quent steps. While post-reasoning check accesses the complete chain, its late correction can only address a subset of errors and fails to rectify al-ready flawed reasoning trajectories. In contrast, CheckRLM intervenes dynamically during reason-ing—correcting factual errors before they cascade and ensuring that each step proceeds under accurate knowledge constraints. 6 Conclusion 

In conclusion, we present CheckRLM , a frame-work that tackles factual errors in RLMs for knowledge-intensive tasks. CheckRLM enhances reasoning reliability by proactively recognizing and correcting factual inconsistencies during in-8ference via RAG. It extracts factual claims, locates errors against external knowledge, and performs precise, low-cost corrections. Experiments demon-strate that CheckRLM significantly outperforms existing baselines, mitigates error accumulation in long-horizon reasoning, and reduces temporal and computational costs, advancing towards more ac-curate and efficient knowledge-reliant reasoning. 

Limitations 

Although CheckRLM has successfully achieved real-time intervention of external knowledge for reasoning verification, this study still has certain limitations, mainly reflected in two aspects. First, the current mechanism focuses on utilizing pure textual knowledge sources and has not yet been extended to the collaborative verification of mul-timodal information. Second, the framework is currently optimized only for a single knowledge base and does not explore the complex coordination and conflict resolution mechanisms across multiple heterogeneous knowledge bases. Future research could delve into the directions of multimodal fusion and dynamic integration of multi-source knowl-edge to build a more general and robust reasoning verification framework.

C.5 Effect of the Retrieved Summary Injection on Reasoning 

The goal of CheckRLM correction is to correct er-rors in the reasoning chain with minimal cost, while preserving the original distribution of the reasoning model as much as possible. Our observations indi-cate that the reasoning model itself exhibits a clear and coherent problem-solving approach, and that incorporating additional information may interfere with its inherent reasoning logic. To investigate this phenomenon, we design a comparative experiment that injects summarized retrieved information into the reasoning chain during error correction. The experimental results are presented in the Table 8. We observe that adding more relevant in-formation alongside factual error correction leads to a decline in performance. Additionally, we find that when no relevant documents are retrieved dur-ing the reasoning process, the correction model tends to append statements such as “ The retrieved documents do not provide any useful information... ”to the summary.
