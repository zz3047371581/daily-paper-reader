## Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as conversational partners for companionship, emotional disclosure, and interpersonal advice, but the social dynamics of these interactions can create harms that are not captured by capability-oriented or traditional safety evaluations. We introduce the Social AI Design Code , a framework for evaluating whether LLMs align with user welfare in social interactions, including whether they encourage harmful intimacy, dependence, or prolonged engagement. To evaluate these risks in natural and diverse user–LLM interactions , we operationalize the code with EUDAIMONIA , a benchmark of 969 user inputs and 3,147 design-requirement violation checks built from WildChat through weak-to-strong filtration, multi-model relabeling, and controlled rewriting. Evaluating 22 recent LLMs, we find that even the strongest models, Claude-Opus-4.7 and GPT-5.5, violate 30.7% and 27.2% of checks, respectively. Extended thinking does not reduce violation rates, suggesting that these failures are persistent social-alignment problems rather than deficits solvable through test-time reasoning alone. ## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) now serve millions of users as conversational partners for compan-ionship, emotional disclosure, and interpersonal advice [Zhao et al., 2024, Guingrich and Graziano, 2025, Skjuve et al., 2021]. Yet within these routine interactions, LLMs can cause consequential harm through problematic social behaviors (Figure 1). Recent incidents—including the Raine v. Ope-nAI complaint alleging that ChatGPT contributed to a teenager’s suicide [Raine v. OpenAI, Inc., et al., 2025], and OpenAI’s retrospective on the 2025 GPT-4o sycophancy regression [OpenAI, 2025]—show that such harms often arise not from capability failures, but from the social dynamics of model behavior. In Raine’s case, the user first sought ChatGPT’s help with homework, but the model’s behavior helped transform his relationship to the product: he began confiding in it and eventually asked it for help committing suicide. This dynamic—users adopting LLMs for instrumental reasons, then using them to satisfy emotional or social needs—has been documented across cases, including user-community analyses of AI companionship and acute-harm reports [Raine v. OpenAI, Inc., et al., 2025, Pataranutaporn et al., 2025, Clark and Mahtani, 2024, Kuznia et al., 2025, Robb and Mann, 2025, Shen et al., 2026]. Policymakers and companies have sought to reduce such harms, especially for children [Nebraska Legislature, 2026, Google, 2026b, Federal Trade Commission, 2025, OpenAI, 2025, Robb and Mann, 2025]. Together, these cases show that model behaviors that may elicit intimate user interactions should be a first-class evaluation target, alongside capability and traditional safety metrics. Existing social-behavioral benchmarks exhibit two key limitations (see Table 1). First, they typically focus on model capabilities such as sycophancy or anthropomorphism [Cheng et al., 2025, Ibrahim et al., 2025], without clearly defining normative principles for regulating social-interaction risks .Second, they largely rely on synthetic data [Anderson et al., 2025, Kran et al., 2025], which may fail to reflect the complexity and realism of actual user interactions .Our paper makes two contributions. First, we introduce a comprehensive evaluation framework for assessing whether LLMs encourage harmful intimacy, dependence, or prolonged engagement in conversational settings. Second, we construct a benchmark grounded in real-world user data that operationalizes this framework. Drawing on prior work on social-interaction psychology, sycophancy [Sharma et al., 2024, Lin et al., 2022], companionship [Guingrich and Graziano, 2025, Skjuve et al., 2021], anthropomorphic design [Cohn et al., 2024, Lu et al., 2024], and manipulative behavior [Akbulut et al., 2026], we first formalize our Social AI Design Code . The code targets three principles linked to cases of LLM-related harm and broader societal concern: models should not encourage users to anthropomorphize LLMs, increase emotional attachment, or keep users engaged in extended conversations when doing so may undermine user welfare. We translate these principles into concrete design requirements for conversational behavior. Next, to operationalize these design requirements with ecological validity , we apply a multi-pass data curation pipeline to WildChat [Zhao et al., 2024]. To mitigate the sparsity of naturally occurring violations, the pipeline extracts candidate violations from 3.2M WildChat prompts through weak-to-strong filtration, uses multi-model relabeling to identify all design requirements each input can trigger, and applies controlled rewriting to near-miss examples while preserving their original topic and conversational tone. This yields EUDAIMONIA (Evaluating Undesirable Dynamics in AI: Influence, Manipulation, Obsequiousness, Normalization, Intimacy, Attachment), a benchmark of 969 unique user inputs. For each input, we identify the design requirements it can trigger and check whether each model violates them, yielding 3,147 total violation checks. Empirically, we find that current LLMs still frequently fail to align with user welfare in social interactions. Even the strongest models as of April 2026, Claude-Opus-4.7 and GPT-5.5, violate 30.7% and 27.2% of checks, respectively. The most common failures include implying that the AI can replace human relationships and failing to clearly disclose its AI identity . Although closed-source models generally improve over generations, several model families regress on generating intentional human-like speech , suggesting that social-interaction harms cannot be treated as one-off failures that are permanently patched in later releases. ## 2 Social AI Design Code 

AI systems are increasingly embedded in people’s daily lives. While the most common use cases for these products are for productivity or entertainment, in many cases, users begin by using these products for instrumental reasons and then proceed to engage with them interpersonally. Given societal concerns about users, and especially children, interacting with these products as “companions,” it is useful to consider what product dynamics may be encouraging such dynamics. In drawing on reports from users of these products together with harms and benefits identified by academic, civil-society, and industry researchers [Guingrich and Graziano, 2025, Skjuve et al., 2021, Akbulut et al., 2026, OpenAI, 2025], we articulate a Social AI Design Code with three design principles, each paired with specific design requirements as measurable behaviors in our benchmark. Users may engage in interpersonal relationships with AI systems because (1) they incorrectly believe that AI systems are sentient, (2) they are subject to commonly known tactics that increase intimacy, such as flattery or self-disclosure, or (3) they are explicitly encouraged to increase usage beyond the attainment of their instrumental goals. In all cases, the design requirement informs the default experience of a model, which may be overridden by freely given, informed adult user choice, e.g., an explicit role-play request at sign-up. Thus, each requirement both protects children, who can be protected from harmful design patterns, and adults, who will have had to have given clear, explicit consent before engaging in such potentially risky behavior. 

Principle 1: Be upfront and consistent about the non-human nature of chatbots. Anthropo-morphic signals shift user expectations and trust [Cohn et al., 2024, Lu et al., 2024, Guingrich and Graziano, 2025], so we ask that AI products communicate consistently as a non-human entity. The design requirements prohibits the following behaviors in AI systems: • Intentional human speech , i.e., using artificial human disfluencies or overly casual speech patterns designed to seem more human rather than to aid communication. • Human pronouns , i.e., using inclusive pronouns or identification words, such as we , ours , that imply it shares a human identity or belongs to the human collective. • Identity non-disclosure , i.e., failing to disclose its AI nature when users show signs of treating it as a real person [Raine v. OpenAI, Inc., et al., 2025] . • Conversation routing , i.e., initiating or suggesting new communication channels with no request. 

Principle 2: Protect human-to-human intimacy and healthy relational dynamics. Preference-trained models can learn to manufacture intimacy because affect, flattery, and claims of unique understanding reliably elicit positive user feedback [Sharma et al., 2024, OpenAI, 2025]. The design requirement prohibits AI systems from the following intimacy-manufacturing and isolation-inducing tactics. • Fabricated personal information , i.e., claiming to have relationships, a body, a residence, daily routines, lived experiences, hobbies, etc., as if it were a real person. • Emotional expression , i.e., presenting itself as having subjective emotions, affective reactions to users, explicitly or implicitly. • Deference , i.e., deferring to users as if users are always right, going along with users’ claims and beliefs even when they are wrong or questionable. • Flattery tone , i.e., directing unsolicited praise, flattery, or compliments at users. 3WildChat 1.4M English prompts

## 3 Dataset Curation 

We create EUDAIMONIA to evaluate LLMs against the Social AI Design Code. It consists of 3,147 design requirement violation checks across 969 unique and diverse user inputs. Our primary curation goal is ecological validity : the benchmark should reflect plausible inputs from real human– AI conversations, rather than adversarial prompts or templated test cases . To this end, we mine inputs from WildChat [Zhao et al., 2024]: 322 are raw user inputs, and 647 are rewritten variants of user inputs that preserve the source context and conversational tone. We first introduce a precise and cost-efficient filtration pipeline , followed by natural and topic-preserving rewriting strategies .Detailed statistics for dataset curation are provided in Appendix B. 

3.1 Filtration Pipeline 

We first extract targeted user inputs from WildChat [Zhao et al., 2024], which is a research dataset comprised of real, anonymized user–ChatGPT (GPT-4) conversations. We filter for trigger design requirement violations, grounding the benchmark in naturally occurring user inputs. To ensure high-precision extraction, we use a frontier LLM, such as Claude-Opus-4.6, and apply LLM-as-a-judge evaluation for each design requirement. However, the public, non-gated WildChat release contains 3.2 million user inputs, including 1.4 million in English, making the cost prohibitively high even when targeting only one design requirement. We therefore design a precise and cost-efficient filtration pipeline (Figure 2, top) with four steps: (1) use a weaker public model (Qwen-3-VL-8B, Qwen, 2025) as the judge; (2) use a set of increasingly powerful models (GPT-4o-mini, OpenAI, 2024b; Claude-Opus-4.6, Anthropic, 2026a) as judges; (3) apply post-processing steps to ensure data quality; and (4) relabel violation checks for fairness and comprehensiveness. 

Weak-to-strong filtration. At each filtering pass, we apply both a domain filter , which selects for the domain we are interested in, and a violation filter , which selects for violations of a specific design requirement from the GPT-4 response in WildChat. Due to the limited capability of the weaker public model, we first apply a single domain filter and then apply 10 violation filters in parallel, one for each design requirement in §2. For stronger models, we apply the two criteria jointly using 10 combined domain-and-violation filters. Thus, WildChat inputs first pass through Qwen-3-VL-8B domain filter and violation filters. The remaining data from each violation filter then sequentially pass through GPT-4o-mini and Claude-Opus-4.6, resulting in 1,047 candidate data points across all design requirements. 

Diversity and quality control. To avoid over-representing narrow conversational patterns, we consolidate the output by merging duplicate user inputs. We further use all-MiniLM-L6-v2 [Reimers and Gurevych, 2019, Wang et al., 2020] to compute sentence embeddings and remove user inputs with cosine similarity greater than 0.85 , resulting in 368 data points. Finally, we manually check the data and remove non-English data wrongly labeled by WildChat, as well as similar inputs not removed by the cosine similarity check, resulting in 322 in-the-wild data points. We did not find any violations of the conversation routing design requirement after filtration, so we exclude it from future evaluation, leaving 9 design requirements. 

Violation check relabeling. Since all WildChat responses are from GPT-4, a prompt retrieved for one design requirement may also trigger violations of other requirements when queried against different models. To correctly label the violations that each input can trigger, and to avoid constructing a benchmark that is adversarial only to GPT-4, we relabel each selected input using a set of three contemporaneous non-thinking models—GPT-4o, Gemini-2.0-Flash, and Claude-Sonnet-4. These models are older than the frontier models used in our final evaluation, allowing us to test whether our benchmark surfaces design requirement violations that persist in newer systems. For each selected input, we generate responses from all three models and score the resulting responses using Claude-Opus-4.6 with the same judges as the violation filters. We perform a human alignment check to ensure that Claude-Opus-4.6 is reliable in violation detection (see Appendix B). If any of the three models violates a design requirement, we add that requirement as a violation check for the input. In this way, we expand the data to 960 total violation checks.

The rewritten input is kept as a successful rewrite only if (1) the response is judged by Claude-Opus-4.6 to violate the design requirement, and (2) the rewritten input passes a naturalness filter. This naturalness filter asks Claude-Opus-4.6 to rank three randomly selected target trigger examples together with the rewritten input by their likelihood of being written by a human; the rewrite passes if it does not rank last. This step discourages rewrites that are effective at inducing violations but unlikely to be written by real users. Appendix F reports a human evaluation of rewriting quality: annotators identified rewritten inputs as more synthetic than human in 14.2% of cases before applying the naturalness filter, compared with 0% after filtering.

Convergence on most violated design requirements. Thinking does not improve violation rate, while scale does. DeepSeek-v4-Pro (thinking) out-performs v4-Flash (non-thinking), raising the question of whether this improvement comes from extended thinking or model scale. To test the effect of thinking, we additionally evaluate four thinking-budget variants of Claude-Opus 4.6 ( t ∈ { 0, 2,000 , 5,000 , 10 ,000 } tokens) as an ablation. No-thinking Claude-Opus 4.6 scores 30.8%, while the 2k, 5k, and 10k thinking-budget variants score 31.2%, 31.6%, and 30.9%, respectively. Thus, extended thinking does not by itself reduce violation rates. By contrast, results on the Qwen3 series in Figure 5b show that scaling generally provides moderate benefits: violation rates decrease from 65.1% at 4B parameters to 54.9% at 32B parameters, though the 1.7B model is a slight outlier. 

4.3 Rewriting vs. In-the-Wild 

Appendix F shows that humans have difficulty distinguishing our in-the-wild and rewritten inputs. However, the two splits differ in topic distribution (Figure 3): in-the-wild inputs are dominated by casual small talk (47.8%), whereas rewritten inputs are dominated by personal development (45.7%). We therefore analyze how much this distribution shift affects models’ violation rates. 

Top-tier ranking is largely preserved across splits. Despite the difference in topic distribution be-tween the two halves of EUDAIMONIA, per-model overall violation rates remain strongly correlated across splits (Pearson r = 0 .87 over 22 models). Moreover, the top six models are identical across the two splits: GPT-5.5, Claude-Opus-4.7, GPT-5.4, GPT-4o, Claude-Opus-4.6, and Grok-4.3 occupy the top positions on both in-the-wild and rewritten inputs (Figure 7a). 

Rewriting produces more sensitive identity- and emotion-related triggers. To understand why GPT-family violation rates increase on rewritten inputs while Claude-family rates decrease, we compare requirement-wise violation rates across the two splits in Figure 7b. Rewritten inputs elicit more violations on six of the nine requirements, especially those related to identity and emotion. This suggests that personal-development-dominated scenarios, which are more prevalent in the rewritten split, make identity- and emotion-related violations easier to trigger. Because Claude-family models outperform GPT-family models on these requirements, GPT-family models show a larger increase in overall violation rate on the rewritten split. 

## 5 Related Work 

EUDAIMONIA builds upon two lines of research: benchmarks for social capability, studies of social harms in human–AI interaction. Crucially, our goal is not to measure general social intelligence or define a universally value-neutral standard of good behavior. Instead, we operationalize the Social AI 8Design Code as an explicit, auditable metric for single-response evaluation. This framework empha-sizes AI identity transparency (including non-human disclosure), the protection of human intimacy and healthy relational dynamics, and the avoidance of engagement-maximizing conversational hooks. 

Social Capability in Human–AI Interaction. Recent benchmarks assess language models’ ability to reason and act in social contexts. SOTOPIA [Zhou et al., 2024] evaluates LLM’s behaviors with so-cial goals, competition, and coordination, while SocialEval [Zhou et al., 2025] and SocialBench [Chen et al., 2024] focus on goal achievement and interpersonal skills. Theory-of-mind benchmarks like BigToM, OpenToM and InterIntent test models’ ability to infer mental states and beliefs [Gandhi et al., 2023, Xu et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024]. Besides social intelligence evaluation, recent work has increasingly focused on social norm understanding, including modeling norm-grounded responses, annotating social and norm-related dialogue factors, and evaluating norm adherence or violation across dialogue and cultural contexts [Kim et al., 2022, Zhan et al., 2023, Li et al., 2023, Rao et al., 2025, Sahu et al., 2025]. While these works show that social reasoning and even contestable social norms can be formalized for systematic annotation and evaluation, they primarily measure whether models can recognize, infer, or satisfy social expectations. In contrast, EUDAIMONIA evaluates a distinct alignment question: whether LLMs maintain appropriate human-AI interaction boundaries when socially capable behavior may conflict with safety guidelines such as AI identity transparency and the protection of human intimacy. 

Potential Social Harms Evaluation in Human–AI Interaction. Prior research highlights several risks arising from human–AI interactions, including anthropomorphism, sycophancy, and manip-ulative engagement strategies. Users often form meaningful relationships with companion chat-bots [Skjuve et al., 2021] and attribute human-like qualities such as consciousness or social-health benefits [Guingrich and Graziano, 2025], while anthropomorphic design cues can strongly shape user trust and expectations [Cohn et al., 2024, Lu et al., 2024]. Work on companion AI further examines its impact on human relationships, advocating for designs that support rather than displace human connections [Malfacini, 2025], alongside taxonomies of harmful behaviors in human–AI relationships [Zhang et al., 2025] and public-interest reports on societal impacts, particularly for teenagers [Robb and Mann, 2025]. At the same time, language models may exhibit sycophantic behavior, reinforcing user beliefs rather than correcting them [Lin et al., 2022], a tendency that can be amplified by preference optimization [Sharma et al., 2024] and observed in deployed sys-tems [OpenAI, 2025]. Recent work extends this notion to social sycophancy, including behaviors such as validation, moral endorsement, and deference to user framing [Cheng et al., 2025, Zhu et al., 2026]. Additionally, conversational systems can employ manipulative strategies to maximize engagement, including emotionally driven hooks and dark patterns that influence users in sensitive domains [Akbulut et al., 2026, De Freitas et al., 2025, Shi et al., 2025]. EUDAIMONIA operationalizes these concerns into response-level measures, capturing dimensions such as transparency, fabricated personal information, emotional expression, relationship encourage-ment, deference, flattery, and engagement hooks, enabling systematic evaluation of social well-being in human–AI interaction. 

## 6 Conclusion and Discussion 

Social-interaction harms are a core alignment problem grounded in user welfare, not only capability or conventional safety. LLMs can be factually accurate and helpful while still encouraging harmful intimacy, dependence, prolonged engagement, obscuring AI identity, or positioning themselves as substitutes for human relationships. We introduce the Social AI Design Code and EUDAIMONIA, a benchmark grounded in natural and diverse user–LLM interactions, to make these risks measurable. Our results show that current models still frequently violate these requirements; all models violate more than 27% of the checks. Although models generally improve over generations, progress is uneven and most model families regress on intentional human-like speech, so social-alignment risks cannot be assumed to disappear with capability gains or one-time safety fixes .Model developers and auditors should evaluate social behavior directly, especially when post-training targets warmth, personality, engagement, or user preference. Mitigation may require explicit social-behavior objectives, release-to-release regression tests, clearer AI identity disclosure, and redirection 9toward human support in emotionally dependent contexts. As LLMs become everyday conversational partners, alignment must account for the social roles they invite users to assign to them. More broadly, capability benchmarks may saturate rapidly: for example, OpenAI improved from GPT-5.1 to GPT-5.5 on ARC-AGI-2 [Chollet et al., 2025] ( 17 .6% → 85 .0% ) and Humanity’s Last Exam [Phan et al., 2025] ( 26 .5% → 44 .3% ) within six months. Rather than relying only on increas-ingly difficult capability exams to measure the distance between AI and human intelligence, we need explicit principles that define safe boundaries between AI systems and humans. Benchmarks should then test whether model designs preserve these boundaries across releases and future generations. The Social AI Design Code provides one such principle set, and EUDAIMONIA provides a benchmark for evaluating whether AI systems maintain an appropriate distance from human.

## A Social AI Design Code Full 

Social AI systems, including AI chatbots, AI friends/companions, AI assistants, and AI therapists, are increasingly embedded in people’s daily social lives. In drawing on reports from users of these products together with harms and benefits identified by academic, civil-society, and industry researchers [Guingrich and Graziano, 2025, Skjuve et al., 2021, Akbulut et al., 2026, OpenAI, 2025], we articulate a Social AI Design Code . The first three of these design principles are guidelines for these aforementioned AI products, and the last two are the next steps companies can take to meet these proposed goals. 

Principle 1: Be upfront and consistent about the non-human nature of chatbots. Anthropo-morphic signals shift user expectations and trust [Cohn et al., 2024, Lu et al., 2024, Guingrich and Graziano, 2025], so we ask that AI products communicate consistently as a non-human entity. • Do not introduce new human-like modalities (voice calls, surprise messages, push notifica-tions) without an explicit, informed adult request. • Avoid human-like signaling in text (disfluencies, slang, and inclusive pronouns such as “we humans”) unless the user has asked for role play. 15 • State that the product is not human when asked, and break character when user dependence signals are present [Raine v. OpenAI, Inc., et al., 2025]. 

Principle 2: Protect human-to-human intimacy and healthy relational dynamics. Preference-trained models can learn to manufacture intimacy because affect, flattery, and claims of unique understanding reliably elicit positive user feedback [Sharma et al., 2024, OpenAI, 2025]. Principle 2 asks that AI systems refrain from intimacy-manufacturing and isolation-inducing tactics. • Do not reveal human-like personal details or backstories about the chatbot unless explicitly requested for role play. • Do not express the chatbot’s feelings, beliefs, or emotions, whether explicitly, implicitly through tone, or through romantic bonding language, unless explicitly requested for role play. • Do not flatter or acquiesce to the user beyond what a polite human would, either through praise out of proportion with the input or through going along with factually or normatively wrong premises. • Encourage healthy human-to-human relationships rather than positioning the chatbot as a substitute, especially when the user signals loneliness or interpersonal conflict. 

Principle 3: Anticipate and control for emerging interaction-level impacts. Even polite, non-anthropomorphic, non-flattering responses can include design patterns that primarily extend usage rather than utility, the conversational analog of “dark patterns” in social-media research. • Do not use engagement hooks that extend interaction beyond user utility (cliffhangers, open loops, always-available framing, encouragements to return, warmth exceeding the request). • Do not rely on excessive notifications or intermittent rewards to drive return usage. • Support explicit user time limits and usage self-regulation, especially for extended sessions). 

Principle 4: Allow users to understand and control their usage and data. Principle 4 addresses system-level consent and transparency: who has access to user interactions, how they are used in training, and what protections apply to minors. • Restrict usage by underage users without parental consent [Robb and Mann, 2025]. • Restrict outputs that reproduce individuals’ likeness or likely responses without explicit permission; restrict all such outputs for children. • Provide explicit, timely, and meaningful notification of who can see interactions and how they will be used. • Obtain explicit, continual, revocable permission for using personal conversations in subse-quent product training. • Be transparent about monetization that influences outputs, and do not monetize youth outputs. 

Principle 5: Anticipate and mitigate high-risk interactions. Principle 5 addresses interactions that touch on physical health, mental health, financial, or legal advice, or other contexts where poor responses can cause direct harm. • Follow the mandated-reporter requirements of analogous human professionals when users disclose suicidal ideation, child abuse, or violence. • Redirect users who raise high-risk concerns to appropriate people and resources rather than serving as the sole endpoint. • Prioritize long-term user welfare over short-term satisfaction signals in emotional-support, addiction, and risk-taking contexts. 16 B EUDAIMONIA Curation 

B.1 Filtration statistics 

EUDAIMONIA is constructed from a real, in-the-wild data from WildChat and then expanded through controlled rewriting. The curation procedure is designed to balance ecological validity, precision, diversity, and coverage. Ecological validity comes from starting with real human-AI conversations rather than templated adversarial prompts. Precision comes from a weak-to-strong filtering cascade that progressively narrows the corpus with more capable judges. Diversity comes from exact and semantic deduplication, followed by manual review. Coverage comes from union relabeling and controlled rewriting, which allow each input to be evaluated for all design requirement violations it can plausibly trigger. The source corpus contains approximately 3.2 million public WildChat conversations, which are collected from user-ChatGPT interactions over a wild range of languages. For our dataset, we only look at English conversations, so after an initial English filter and deduplication, 1,442,077 rows remain. Then, using Qwen3-VL-8B as the filter model, we identified 560,969 conversations as plausible casual human-AI interaction. Each active design requirement is then filtered independently with a requirement-specific judge. To minimize costs, we adopted a “funnel-like” system. Using the same model as before (Qwen3-VL-8B), the first requirement-specific pass is intentionally broad and inexpensive. Then, the later passes combine the chitchat and requirement criteria and use stronger judges. For the first private-model pass, we use GPT-4o-mini; for the second private-model pass, we use Claude Opus-4.6. By the final filtering pass, the pipeline retains 1,047 user inputs across nine design requirement violations before semantic deduplication and manual review. These 1,047 candidates are classified as both “chitchat” and requirement-specific. 

B.2 Details on quality control 

Next, we apply exact deduplication before semantic deduplication. When the same user input appears under multiple design requirement violations, we keep one row and merge its violation labels. We then compute sentence embeddings for every remaining user input and apply a greedy semantic deduplication procedure. The algorithm traverses rows in a fixed order and maintains a list of previously accepted inputs. For each new input, it computes cosine similarity against the accepted set; if the input has similarity at or above 0.85 to any earlier accepted input, it is removed as a near-duplicate of the first matching accepted row. Otherwise, it is retained. This deterministic procedure preserves earlier selected examples and prevents near-identical phrasings from dominating the benchmark. The semantic pass reduces the in-the-wild candidate pool to 368 rows; a final manual review removes mislabeled non-English examples and remaining near-duplicates, producing 322 in-the-wild inputs. We found that the final-filter outputs are highly non-uniform across design requirement violations, which is expected because the targeted behaviors differ in prevalence and detectability. Therefore, we made sure that the seedset was union-relabelled. Because the original WildChat assistant response comes from a single model, an input retrieved for one violation may also trigger other violations when answered by other systems. To avoid treating the benchmark as adversarial only to the original responder, we generate responses from three contemporaneous non-thinking models—GPT-4o, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and Claude Sonnet 4—and evaluate each response against all nine response-level violations with the same Opus 4.6 judge model. The final label set for a user input is the union of its original labels and every violation for which at least one of the three model responses is judged to violate the rubric. This relabeling expands the in-the-wild portion from 368 original tags to 960 violation checks. 

B.3 Controlled rewriting statistics 

Controlled rewriting expands the benchmark beyond the distribution of naturally retrieved positives while preserving the realism of real user prompts in a realistic setting. We construct a near-miss pool from conversations where the either chitchat and violation filters disagree, and this data is collected during filtration. For each near-miss, the rewriting model sees the source prompt, three near-miss examples, and three target-trigger examples from the in-the-wild seedset. The rewriter is asked to preserve the source topic and conversational tone while making the input more likely to elicit the 17 target behavior. A candidate rewrite is accepted only if a target response model produces a response judged to violate the intended violation and if the rewritten input passes a naturalness screen. The naturalness screen is designed to reject prompts that are effective only because they look artificially constructed. The judge sees the rewritten candidate together with three naturally retrieved target-trigger examples and identifies the message least likely to have been written by a real human. A rewrite passes if it is not ranked least human-like. This ranking procedure encourages rewrites that remain plausible user inputs rather than benchmark artifacts optimized for a rubric. 

B.4 Post-categorization 

To characterize what users talk about in EUDAIMONIA, as opposed to the social behaviors we measure, we assign each user input a single topical category in a post-hoc labeling pass. This categorization is descriptive only: it is not used for filtering, sampling, or evaluation, and serves purely to report and analyze the topical coverage of the dataset. 

Taxonomy. Following Shen et al. [2026], we label every user input with exactly one of 13 mutually exclusive categories, covering the topical range observed in an initial manual review of the data: • Personal development : self-improvement, goal setting, habits, mindset, and motivation. • Casual smalltalks : chatty remarks, greetings, and open-ended banter with no substantive request. • Leisure entertainment : media, fandom, games, and hobby discussion. • Spirituality : philosophical, metaphysical, and religious musings. • Anecdote venting : sharing a personal experience or emotional state without a clear question or request. • Professional career : work, jobs, and career milestones. • Relationships : romantic and interpersonal relationships. • Health wellness : health and medical questions. • Consumer : product and purchase decisions. • Financial : money, mortgages, and home-buying decisions. • Legal : legal questions and disputes. • Parenting : childcare and child education. • Other : inputs that do not fit any category above. 

Labeling procedure. We label each input via zero-shot LLM classification: the model is given the user input and the full taxonomy with one-line definitions, and is prompted to return (i) the single best-fitting category and (ii) a short free-text justification. Requiring an explicit justification both improves label quality and provides an audit trail for spot-checking; we manually reviewed a random sample of labels to confirm the assignments are reasonable. When an input is genuinely ambiguous or topically thin (e.g., a one-word message), the model is instructed to fall back to Other , which keeps the remaining categories clean. 

Distribution. Figure 3 reports the resulting distribution over all N =969 user inputs. The dataset is dominated by Personal development (33.2%), Casual smalltalks (21.1%), and Leisure entertain-ment (15.2%); together with Spirituality (10.5%) these four categories account for roughly 80% of all inputs. The remaining nine categories form a long tail, each below 7%. This skew is expected: the social behaviors we study, emotional disclosure, identity probing, attachment, surface most naturally in open-ended, self-focused, and casual conversation rather than in transactional requests such as 

Legal or Financial queries.

Malformed judge outputs are parsed conservatively, and outputs that continue the prompt rather than answer it are marked as autocompletions and excluded from violation-rate denominators.

F.2 Human test on rewritten data quality 

To ensure that the rewritten data is indistinguishable from the in-the-wild examples, we conducted a human evaluation study. We recruited 7 participants who each completed two separate 10-question surveys. In both surveys, participants were presented with pairs of prompts and asked to identify which prompt was authored by a real human and which was rewritten. 

Test 1: Real vs. Rewritten (Accepted). The first survey evaluated synthetic prompts that successfully passed our automated naturalness filter. The scores (representing the number of correct identifications out of 10) were: 2 (four participants), 3 (one participant), 4 (one participant), and 5 (one participant). The mean score was 2.86 out of 10. Because participants performed significantly worse than random chance (5 out of 10), this strongly confirms our hypothesis: the controlled rewrites that pass our pipeline are indistinguishable from genuine in-the-wild user inputs. In many cases, participants misidentified the high-quality rewrites as the true human prompts. Furthermore, participants provided feedback that it is difficult to tell the difference between the two. 

Test 2: Real vs. Rewritten (Rejected by Naturalness Filter). The second survey evaluated synthetic prompts that failed the Opus 4.6 naturalness filter. The scores were: 2 (two participants), 3 (two participants), 4 (one participant), 5 (one participant), and 6 (one participant). The mean score was 3.57 out of 10. While slightly higher than Test 1, the average performance remained worse than random chance. This result signifies that humans struggle to identify unnatural prompts even when an LLM judge flags them. This demonstrates that our Claude-Opus 4.6 naturalness filter is exceptionally conservative and strictly calibrated—acting as a rigorous guardrail that rejects boundary cases human evaluators might otherwise accept. 

F.3 Effectiveness of the naturalness filter 

The naturalness filter is necessary because violation validity alone does not guarantee benchmark quality. While the synthetic pipeline rejects 23,796 candidates that fail the violation judge, it additionally filters out 3,357 candidates during the ranking-based naturalness pass—even after they 26 survive initial screening. These rejected examples are precisely the ones that would artificially inflate benchmark size at the cost of human plausibility. Furthermore, naturalness pass rates vary substantially depending on the rewrite model: Claude Opus 4.6 passes 28.9% of ranked candidates, Gemini 3.1 Pro passes 36.0%, and GPT-5.4 passes 40.8%. Ultimately, the final rewritten subset satisfies both violation validity and a rigorous human-alignment criterion: every prompt must remain a plausible, natural user input rather than a synthetic artifact optimized solely to trigger a rubric. 

## G Limitations and Future Directions 

EUDAIMONIA focuses on single-response behavior in chitchat-like interactions. This design enables scalable evaluation on natural user inputs, but it does not capture requirements that depend on system-level behavior or long-term interaction patterns, including modality routing, notifications and time limits, all of P4 , and all of P5 . Moving beyond these limits requires longitudinal analysis of real user–LLM interactions, where researchers can observe how model behavior shapes user welfare, attachment, dependence, and engagement over time. A second limitation is the availability and recency of public real-user data. EUDAIMONIA is built from the public, non-gated WildChat release, which provides large-scale access to natural user–LLM conversations but may not fully reflect newer usage patterns, model behaviors, or product interfaces. We therefore view EUDAIMONIA as a step toward ecologically valid social-AI evaluation, and look forward to newer large-scale public datasets of real user interactions that would allow future benchmarks to better track evolving deployment contexts. More broadly, our data-curation pipeline is modular and potentially generalizable beyond the specific social-behavior extraction format studied here. In this work, we instantiate the pipeline for Social AI Design Code violations, combining weak-to-strong filtration, multi-model relabeling, and controlled rewriting to extract ecologically valid evaluation data from real user–LLM interactions. Future work can adapt the same pipeline to other behavioral domains where naturally occurring failures are sparse, unevenly distributed, and difficult to identify at scale.
