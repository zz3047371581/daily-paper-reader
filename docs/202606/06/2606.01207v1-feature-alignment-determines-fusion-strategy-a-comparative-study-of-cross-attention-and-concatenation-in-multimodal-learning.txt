# Abstract 

The choice between cross-attention and concatenation for multimodal fusion remains governed by practitioner intuition rather than principled understanding. In this paper, we demonstrate that feature alignment qual-ity —not data scale alone—is the primary determinant of which fusion strategy excels. Through controlled experiments on Flickr8k using two feature extraction backbones (ResNet18 and CLIP ViT-B/32), we show that concatenation outperforms cross-attention by 4.1– 5.1 percentage points across all tested scales (2048– 16384 samples) when features are pre-aligned by a vision-language pretraining objective. We provide a theoreti-cal explanation grounded in sample complexity analysis: concatenation requires O(dv +dt) samples to learn its fu-sion projection, while cross-attention requires O(dv · dt)

samples to learn bilinear attention weights—a 256 × dif-ference for 512-dimensional CLIP features. When fea-tures are already aligned, the approximation error gap between the two methods vanishes, and concatenation’s sample efficiency dominates at all practical dataset sizes. An alignment degradation study confirms a monotonic trend: as feature alignment degrades, concatenation’s advantage grows from 1.3% to 2.8%. These findings pro-vide a principled decision framework for fusion method selection in multimodal systems, with direct implications for the design of Multimodal Large Language Models. 

Keywords: Multimodal fusion, cross-attention, con-catenation, feature alignment, CLIP, scaling laws, sam-ple complexity 

# 1 Introduction 

## 1.1 Background 

Multimodal learning has become a central paradigm in artificial intelligence, enabling systems to process and integrate information from vision and language [Baltru-saitis et al., 2019, Ngiam et al., 2011, Radford et al., 

> ∗Corresponding author.

2021]. The fusion of visual and textual features under-pins a wide range of tasks: image-text retrieval [Faghri et al., 2018], visual question answering [Antol et al., 2015], image captioning [Vinyals et al., 2015], and Mul-timodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) [Liu et al., 2023, Bai et al., 2023, Chen et al., 2024]. Two dominant fusion strategies exist. Concatena-tion fusion concatenates visual and textual feature vectors and applies a learnable linear projection [Kiros et al., 2014, Wang et al., 2016]. Cross-attention fusion 

computes content-dependent attention weights between modalities, enabling dynamic feature selection [Vaswani et al., 2017, Lu et al., 2019, Tan and Bansal, 2019]. Despite their widespread adoption, the choice between them remains heuristic—guided by task conventions, computational budgets, or empirical trial-and-error on a single dataset. 

## 1.2 The Missing Question 

Recent work on scaling laws has transformed our under-standing of neural network behavior [Kaplan et al., 2020, Clark et al., 2021, McCandlish et al., 2018, Hoffmann et al., 2022]. Kaplan et al. [Kaplan et al., 2020] estab-lished power-law relationships between performance and resources for language models. Hoffmann et al. [Hoff-mann et al., 2022] derived compute-optimal training recipes. However, these scaling laws have been studied primarily in unimodal settings. The behavior of mul-timodal fusion as a function of data scale and feature quality remains poorly understood. What is missing is a principled framework for an-swering a practical question: Given a multimodal dataset and a feature extraction pipeline, should I use cross-attention or concatenation?

# 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 Multimodal Fusion Strategies 

Concatenation-based fusion has been widely adopted in architectures such as VSE++ [Faghri et al., 2018] and SCAN [Lee et al., 2018], where visual and textual fea-tures are concatenated and processed by fully connected layers. Cross-attention-based fusion emerged with the Transformer architecture [Vaswani et al., 2017], with notable examples including ViLBERT [Lu et al., 2019], LXMERT [Tan and Bansal, 2019], and the fusion mod-ules in MLLMs such as LLaVA [Liu et al., 2023] and Qwen-VL [Bai et al., 2023]. Hybrid approaches have also been proposed. BLIP-2 [Li et al., 2023] uses a Querying Transformer (Q-Former) as a lightweight bridge between frozen image encoders and frozen LLMs. These approaches typically use a fixed fusion strategy regardless of the data regime. 

## 2.2 Attention Mechanisms 

The Transformer’s self-attention mechanism [Vaswani et al., 2017] has quadratic complexity in sequence length, motivating research on efficient attention vari-ants. Sparse attention methods [Child et al., 2019, Za-heer et al., 2020, Kitaev et al., 2020] reduce complex-ity by attending to a subset of tokens. Linear attention methods [Katharopoulos et al., 2020, Choromanski et al., 2021] replace the softmax kernel with alternative for-mulations. Local attention methods [Dosovitskiy et al., 2021, Liu et al., 2021] restrict attention to local windows. Our work differs in focus: rather than designing new attention mechanisms, we study when standard cross-attention is the right choice versus simpler concatena-tion. 

## 2.3 Scaling Laws 

Scaling laws describe the relationship between model performance and resources [Kaplan et al., 2020, Clark et al., 2021, McCandlish et al., 2018, Hoffmann et al., 2022]. In the multimodal setting, scaling laws have been studied for pretraining [Fang et al., 2023, Sun et al., 2023] but not for fusion method comparison. Our work fills this gap by providing both empirical evidence and theo-retical analysis of how fusion method advantage depends on feature alignment quality. 

## 2.4 Gaps in Existing Work 

Existing comparisons of fusion methods suffer from three limitations: (1) single-scale evaluation, making it impos-sible to determine scale-dependence; (2) lack of theoreti-cal grounding for why one method outperforms another; (3) fixed fusion strategies regardless of the data or fea-ture regime. Our work addresses all three.

# 4 Experimental Setup 

## 4.1 Dataset 

We use the Flickr8k dataset [Hodosh et al., 2013], which contains 8,000 images paired with 5 descriptive captions each. We construct a balanced binary classification task: 

• Positive pairs: An image paired with its correct cap-tion 

• Negative pairs: An image paired with a randomly selected caption from a different image 

• Balance: 50% positive, 50% negative We evaluate at four scales: 2048, 4096, 8192, and 16384 samples. The 16384-scale is the maximum achiev-able with balanced sampling from Flickr8k’s 40,000 image-caption pairs. 

## 4.2 Feature Extraction 

We extract features using two backbone models with fun-damentally different alignment properties: 

ResNet18 features (unaligned): Image features: 512-dimensional (from ResNet18 pretrained on Ima-geNet). Text features: 512-dimensional (from a separate text encoder). These features have no cross-modal alignment —the image and text embedding spaces are independently learned. 

CLIP ViT-B/32 features (pre-aligned): Image features: 512-dimensional (from CLIP’s image encoder). Text features: 512-dimensional (from CLIP’s text en-coder). These features are pre-aligned by CLIP’s contrastive pretraining objective, which maps matching image-text pairs to nearby points in a shared embedding space.

# 7 Discussion 

## 7.1 Implications for MLLM Design 

Our findings have direct implications for the design of Multimodal Large Language Models: 1. When fine-tuning with pre-trained features 

(e.g., CLIP encoder + LLM), concatenation-style fu-sion (e.g., linear projection as in LLaVA [Liu et al., 2023]) should be preferred. The features are al-ready aligned by the pretraining objective, and cross-attention’s extra capacity is wasted. 2. When training from scratch on raw features (e.g., ResNet + LSTM), cross-attention should be pre-ferred. The features are not aligned, and cross-attention’s capacity to learn alignment is essential. 3. Progressive fusion strategies that start with con-catenation (for sample efficiency) and transition to cross-attention (for capacity) as data scales up could offer the best of both worlds.

## 7.3 Limitations 

Our work has several limitations: (1) We evaluated pri-marily on Flickr8k (8,000 images, 40,000 image-caption pairs). Experiments on larger datasets (COCO, Visual 6Genome) would strengthen the findings. (2) We focus on binary image-text matching. Extension to retrieval, VQA, and captioning is needed. (3) We tested ResNet18 and CLIP ViT-B/32. Other combinations (DINOv2, BLIP, EVA-CLIP) would improve generalizability. (4) Our noise-based degradation is a simplification. Real-world misalignment has structure that Gaussian noise does not capture. (5) We evaluated feature-level fusion only. The conclusions may differ in end-to-end settings where the feature extractors are jointly optimized with the fusion module. (6) Our sample complexity analysis uses simplified PAC-learning bounds. Tighter statistical learning theory analysis is needed. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We have shown that feature alignment quality—not data scale alone—is the primary determinant of fusion strategy advantage in multimodal learning. With pre-aligned CLIP features, concatenation outperforms cross-attention by 4.1–5.1% at all tested scales (2048–16384 samples), contradicting the common assumption that cross-attention is universally superior. The theoretical explanation is straightforward: con-catenation has O(dv +dt) sample complexity while cross-attention has O(dv · dt). When features are already aligned, the approximation error gap between the two methods vanishes, and concatenation’s 256 × sample ef-ficiency advantage dominates. An alignment degradation study confirms a monotonic relationship: as feature alignment degrades from perfect (σ = 0 ) to moderate ( σ = 0 .10 ), concatenation’s advan-tage grows from 1.3% to 2.8%. This provides practical guidelines for practitioners: use concatenation when fea-tures are pre-aligned (e.g., by CLIP or BLIP pretrain-ing), and use cross-attention when features are unaligned and sufficient data is available to learn the alignment. 

Future work includes: (1) extending experiments to larger datasets (COCO, Visual Genome) and more tasks (retrieval, VQA); (2) developing adaptive fusion mech-anisms that automatically adjust to feature alignment quality; (3) validating findings with end-to-end train-ing in MLLMs; and (4) exploring whether the feature alignment framework applies to other modalities (audio, video, depth).
