Abstract 

Emotion understanding is a core capability for LLMs to interact effectively with humans, yet existing evaluation paradigms rely on discrete emotion label predic-tion and fail to capture the cognitive processes underlying emotion generation. Grounded in appraisal theory, we introduce CAREBench, the first benchmark with complete inferential chain annotations from both first- and third-person perspec-tives on real-world narratives, spanning appraisal reasoning, appraisal ratings, and multi-label emotion annotation. We propose a process-level evaluation framework and conduct systematic experiments across six LLMs organized around four re-search questions. We find that stronger models match or surpass human observers on certain tasks, yet fall short on appraisal reasoning and positive emotion recog-nition; performance across chain steps and sensitivity to appraisal interventions exhibit dissociations across models; and current models have not internalized the mechanisms needed to capture human subjective heterogeneity. These findings suggest that downstream emotion prediction metrics may overestimate LLMs’ true emotion understanding, and CAREBench provides a foundation for more diagnostically informative evaluation of LLMs’ affective cognitive capabilities. 

1 Introduction 

Emotion understanding is a fundamental capability for AI systems built on Large Language Models (LLMs) deployed in affective computing, human-AI interaction, and mental health support. However, evaluating this capability remains challenging due to the complexity of how emotions are elicited, experienced, and expressed in humans. Most prior work has assessed emotion understanding through discrete emotion label prediction. However, this fails to capture the richness of real-world emotional phenomena [ 1 ]. To address this limitation, recent work has turned to psychological theories of emotion. Among these, appraisal theory posits that emotions arise from individuals’ subjective evaluations of events along multiple dimensions — such as goal relevance and coping potential — providing a structured and interpretable representation for evaluating emotion understanding

Under this perspective, recent studies have introduced appraisal-grounded datasets to examine whether LLMs exhibit cognitive emotion understanding capabilities beyond label prediction [ 3, 4 , 5]. However, existing appraisal-grounded datasets exhibit three key limitations. First, they predominantly treat appraisal dimension ratings as the sole representation of cognitive appraisal. However, much of appraisal involves inferences over mental states, such as beliefs and desires, while appraisal ratings reflect only its outcomes. Without explicitly modeling this inference process, it remains unclear whether a model truly understands the experiencer’s mental states or merely learns a superficial map-ping from situations to ratings. Second, most datasets rely on single emotion category labels, whereas real-world emotional experiences are often mixed and involve multiple co-occurring emotions; thus, a single label fails to capture this richness. Third, the majority of datasets collect only third-person annotations, making it difficult to examine how perspective differences between experiencers and observers systematically influence emotion judgments. While the Crowd-Envent dataset [ 3] includes both first- and third-person perspectives, it contains very short situation descriptions (on average 20 words) and provides only appraisal dimension ratings. As a result, it cannot capture variation in annotators’ underlying appraisal inference processes. 

Figure 1: Overview of the data annotation pipeline. To address these gaps, we constructed CAREBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate Cognitive Appraisal Reasoning in Emotion understanding, with three key features (Figure 1). First, we explic-itly annotated the reasoning process of appraisals . Specifically, we recruited annotators to provide real-life emotional narratives (95 words on average), followed by three annotation layers collected in sequence: open-ended reasoning texts targeting five core appraisal dimensions, hierarchical appraisal ratings across 22 sub-dimensions, and emotion annotations. This design enables the construction of a situation → reasoning → appraisal → emotion inferential chain, making it possible to observe mental-state layer of appraisal, analyze its influence on fine-grained appraisal ratings, and evaluate LLMs’ emotion understanding correctness at the process level. Probing mental states before appraisal ratings also scaffolds the annotation process itself, ensuring that ratings are grounded in explicit reasoning rather than intuitive impression. To our knowledge, no existing dataset elicits mental states as a precursor to appraisal annotation, in either first-person or third-person settings. Second, for emotion annotation, instead of representing emotion as a single discrete label, we allow multi-label emotion annotation with intensity . This supports many-to-many analysis of appraisal–emotion mappings and more faithful modeling of complex real-world emotional responses. In addition, we provide full inferential-chain annotations from both the first-person experiencer and multiple third-person observers , enabling direct comparison between LLMs and both perspectives, as well as localization of subjective heterogeneity along the whole process. Annotator demographics and personality traits are also collected to support subjectivity analysis. Based on this dataset, we conducted systematic evaluations of six LLMs organized around four progressively structured research questions: how well models perform each inferential chain step, whether injecting appraisal reasoning helps with downstream prediction, whether models exhibit directional sensitivity to structured appraisal interventions, and whether they can capture the distri-butional heterogeneity of human appraisal judgments. Our key findings are: (1) although stronger 2models achieve competitive downstream performance, appraisal reasoning capabilities remain limited and positive emotion identification is universally challenging; (2) capabilities across chain steps are dissociable among models, with upstream advantages not consistently transferring downstream; (3) emotion prediction performance does not fully align with sensitivity to appraisal interventions, suggesting some models rely on associative rather than appraisal-based reasoning; and (4) current models have not internalized the mechanisms needed to capture subjective heterogeneity. The main contributions of this work are as follows: • Dataset. We introduce CAREBench, a new benchmark for evaluating LLMs’ emotion under-standing, supplying data absent in existing datasets on explicit appraisal reasoning, multi-emotion co-occurrence, and dual-perspective emotion understanding. • Evaluation framework. We propose a process-level evaluation framework covering the full situation → reasoning → appraisal → emotion inferential chain, moving beyond the single-step, single-perspective evaluation paradigms in existing work. This framework exposes the blind spots of relying solely on downstream emotion prediction performance to assess LLMs’ emotion understanding, providing methodological support for more reliable evaluation of LLMs’ affective cognitive capabilities. • Empirical findings. Through systematic evaluation of six LLMs, we provide an empirical basis for comprehensively diagnosing current LLMs’ emotion understanding capabilities, revealing the insufficiency of existing evaluation paradigms in distinguishing surface-level prediction perfor-mance from genuine appraisal understanding, with direct implications for building more cognitively interpretable affective models in the future. 

2 The CAREBench Dataset 

2.1 Data Collection 

Process models of appraisal theory characterize emotion generation as a multi-stage sequential process, spanning a complete cognitive chain from situation construal through appraisal to emotional response [ 6, 7]. Grounded in this theoretical perspective, we collected annotations covering an inferential chain s → r → a → e, where s denotes the situational narrative, r the open-ended appraisal reasoning text, a the appraisal rating vector, e the emotion annotation. In collaboration with psychology experts, we designed a two-phase data collection protocol, with participants recruited via the Prolific platform. In the first phase, participants provided first-person emotional narratives and completed self-annotation; in the second phase, independent third-party annotators read these narratives and completed annotation from an observer’s perspective. 

Phase 1: First-Person Narratives and Annotation In Phase 1, each participant was asked to recall and describe a recent emotionally significant event. To ensure richness of content while reducing participant burden, we adopted the micro-narratives framework [ 8], using an LLM (GPT-4o) to assist participants in composing their narratives. Specifically, participants engaged in a structured conversation with an AI chatbot, answering a series of guided questions; the LLM then assembled the resulting text by combining participants’ responses in their own words with minimal reformulation, which participants reviewed and corrected as needed. Participants subsequently completed a three-layer annotation task: (1) appraisal reasoning ( r): open-ended responses to questions targeting five core appraisal dimensions (relevance, certainty, congruence, control, and accountability), also completed via the micro-narratives framework; (2) appraisal ratings ( a ∈ R22 ): 5-point Likert-scale ratings on 22 appraisal items derived through hierarchical expansion of the five core dimensions; (3) emotion annotation ( e): participants rated positive and negative emotion intensity e+

2.2 Data Statistics 

The dataset comprises 1,000 first-person annotated samples, 500 of which are additionally annotated from the third-person perspective by 5 independent annotators each, yielding 2,500 third-person annotation records in total (Table 1). The average story length is 95.1 tokens; with core appraisal reasoning text included, the average reaches approximately 160 tokens, with third-person annotators producing slightly shorter inference texts than their first-person counterparts. In terms of appraisal rating distributions (Figure 2), the two annotator groups exhibit broadly similar patterns, though notable polarity shifts emerge on certain dimensions. For instance, third-person annotators tend to overestimate event urgency on A2 ( relevance.urgency ). At the dataset level, most appraisal dimensions show relatively balanced rating distributions, while a few skew markedly toward one pole: A1 ( relevance.general ) is consistently high, indicating that most situations are perceived as highly relevant to the protagonist, whereas A17 ( control.vicarious ) is consistently low, reflecting a widespread perception that others are unlikely to intervene in the current situation. Regarding emotion annotations (Table 1 & Figure 3), negative-emotion samples outnumber positive ones by approximately 1.6-fold, reflecting the natural skew of emotional narratives. Label co-occurrence is prevalent, with most samples receiving 1–4 labels; positive samples carry more labels on average than negative ones, suggesting that positive contexts more readily elicit mixed emotions. Third-person observers tend to assign fewer emotion labels overall, and their rated intensity for negative emotions is slightly lower than that of first-person experiencers, while positive emotion intensity shows a more dispersed distribution. High-frequency emotion categories are highly consistent across perspectives, yet third-person prevalence is lower than first-person for the vast majority of categories, with only a handful of labels ( lonely , panicked , guilty , confused ) being relatively more salient from the third-person perspective. 43 Experimental Setup 

3.1 Models 

We evaluate six models spanning three categories. For closed-source models, we include GPT-5.2 (GPT) [ 10 ] and Claude-Sonnet-4.6 (Claude) [ 11 ]. For open-source general-purpose models, we include Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct (Llama) [ 12 ] and Qwen 3.5-9B (Qwen) [ 13 ]. To examine whether task-specific training on psychology- or emotion-related data confers advantages on appraisal-grounded emotion understanding, we include PsyLLM [ 14 ], fine-tuned from Qwen 3-8B on synthetic mental health counseling dialogues derived from Reddit posts, and EmoLLaMa-chat-13B (EmoLlama) [ 15 ], an instruction-tuned model built on LLaMA2-chat-13B using affective analysis data. More reproducibility details are provided in Appendix A.4. 

3.2 Evaluation Metrics 

Our evaluation framework mirrors the structure of the emotion inferential chain, with metrics defined at each step from situation construal to emotion output, supplemented by measures of subjectivity modeling. Specifically, we evaluate 1) Core appraisal reasoning : since this task requires open-ended text generation, we report BLEU, ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, ROUGE-L, and BERTScore; 2) Appraisal prediction : we compute normalized root mean square error (RMSE) for each appraisal item, where ratings are on a 5-point Likert scale; 3) Emotion intensity prediction : we likewise use normalized RMSE, where intensity ratings are on a 7-point Likert scale; 4) Emotion label prediction : we report example-F1 (per-instance F1 averaged across the dataset), micro-F1 (micro-averaged across labels), and macro-F1 (macro-averaged across labels); 5) Subjectivity modeling : since appraisal maps emotion understanding onto a measurable low-dimensional space, providing a convenient medium for assessing subjective heterogeneity, all metrics here are defined over appraisal dimensions. Following Zhou et al. [ 16 ], we use μ-MAE, σ-MAE, and Wasserstein distance to measure the discrepancy between the model-simulated and human annotator appraisal distributions. We additionally introduce Spearman correlation and precision@ k to assess whether models can identify which appraisal dimensions are more prone to subjective disagreement. 

3.3 Research Questions and Experimental Design RQ1: How well do LLMs perform each step of the inference chain? 

We evaluate LLM prediction performance at each node of the inferential chain under zero-shot settings, covering three tasks: inferring appraisal reasoning text ( s → r), predicting appraisal ratings (s → a), and predicting emotion outputs ( s → e). In each task, models are prompted to put themselves in the shoes of the experiencer, as human third-person observers are instructed to do, and generate predictions accordingly. Model outputs are then compared against human third-person annotations on the same tasks, identifying systematic divergences between model and human observer performance at each step. 

RQ2: Does the appraisal reasoning process help improve appraisal prediction and emotion recognition? 

Appraisal theory posits that the relationship between situational information and emotional outcomes is mediated by structured cognitive appraisals, which has been widely validated in studies on human participants [ 17 , 18 ]. The role of appraisal in LLM-based emotion recognition has also been preliminarily explored in prior work [ 3 , 19 , 20 ]. Given this, we examine whether appraisal reasoning 

r, open-ended text assumed to be more accessible to LLMs, could improve LLMs’ performance on appraisal prediction and emotion recognition. Specifically, we take situational narrative, s, as the baseline condition and design four augmented input conditions: s + r, s + a, s + r + a, and s + ˆ r,where ˆr denotes model-generated appraisal reasoning. We compare ∆P (a) and ∆P (e) across the four conditions, where P (·) denotes the corresponding evaluation metric and ∆ denotes the change relative to the baseline s, assessing whether and how reasoning text improve downstream predictions relative to a and ˆr. Further, for each appraisal dimension we inject dimension-specific reasoning text 

rk into s and examine its differential effects on various dimensions of a and categories of e, testing whether rk yields targeted improvements on the certain appraisals and emotions.

4 Results and Analysis 

RQ1: How well do LLMs perform each step of the inference chain? 

Table 2 summarizes step-by-step performance across the inferential chain. Overall, LLMs perform comparably to or better than human third-person observers on appraisal rating, emotion intensity prediction, and negative emotion label identification, while falling short on appraisal reasoning and positive emotion label identification. For positive emotion label identification, models tend to exhibit greater imbalance between precision and recall compared to human observers, resulting in substantially lower Example F1 scores. For core appraisal reasoning, we combine automatic metrics with human evaluation for a comprehensive assessment: most models score below the human third-person observer on automatic metrics and best-response selection, yet achieve higher consistency scores in human evaluation — a divergence reflecting that human observers tend to draw on personal experience and produce more concise responses, making them more likely to be preferred when the reasoning is relatively unambiguous, whereas models tend toward thorough situational interpretation and generate richer responses, which are more frequently selected when partial alignment is present (see Appendix A.5.1 for full human evaluation results). Across models, Claude and GPT consistently rank at the top across most tasks, including appraisal reasoning. Domain-specific fine-tuned models, however, present a notable dissociation: despite PsyLLM’s strength on appraisal rating and EmoLLaMa’s lead on appraisal reasoning metrics, nei-ther shows a corresponding advantage on downstream emotion label selection, suggesting that upstream inferential chain gains do not necessarily transfer to downstream tasks, and that strong downstream performance does not imply correct appraisal-mediated reasoning. 

We further examine fine-grained results at the appraisal dimension and emotion category levels (Appendix A.5.1). On both appraisal reasoning and appraisal rating, models face the greatest difficulty on the accountability , control , and certainty dimensions, while performing relatively well on relevance . For emotion labels, disgust is the most consistently underperformed negative category, while hopeful , grateful , and inspiration are systematically missed in positive emotion identification, with recall substantially lagging precision. RQ4: Do LLMs capture the distributional heterogeneity of human appraisal judgments? 

Table 4 reports results on models’ ability to approximate human appraisal rating distributions and reproduce inter-annotator disagreement patterns. For distribution fitting, injecting core appraisal reasoning yields the most consistent improvements across all models, with notable reductions in mean MAE and Wasserstein distance; persona injection shows unstable effects, and combining both types is not always optimal. For disagreement pattern reproduction, Spearman ρ and P @k remain close to random baseline, with consistent but modest improvements from reasoning injection and little benefit from persona. Further analysis of output variance reveals that reasoning injection substantially increases variance in most models, yet σ-MAE and disagreement reproduction remain well below human levels, suggesting that models can produce differentiated ratings but through mechanisms that likely differ from human inter-annotator disagreement. 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

This paper presents a process-level evaluation framework for LLM emotion understanding grounded in appraisal theory, and introduces the first dataset with complete dual-perspective inferential chain annotations covering both first- and third-person perspectives on real-world narratives. Through systematic experiments across six LLMs, we find that LLMs achieve or surpass human observer performance on certain steps of the inferential chain, yet exhibit consistent shortfalls in appraisal reasoning generation and positive emotion recognition. Furthermore, while first-person reasoning has a substantial effect on model understanding, model-generated reasoning fails to improve and not all models show sensitivity to appraisal reasoning intervention. Additionally, existing models remain unable to capture the distributional variation in emotion understanding observed across human annotators. These findings suggest that evaluating LLMs along the full inferential chain reveals capability gaps that emotion prediction metrics alone would miss, offering a more diagnostically informative foundation for assessing LLMs’ emotion understanding. Future work may proceed along several directions. First, the present framework characterizes model capabilities along the inferential chain at the behavioral level, while future work may apply mechanistic interpretability methods to investigate how appraisal reasoning is encoded in models’ internal representations. Second, the multi-perspective and multi-label annotation structure of the dataset provides a foundation for modeling emotion as a situation-conditioned distribution, opening up avenues for learning continuous emotion embeddings that better capture subjective heterogeneity in emotion understanding. Third, several limitations identified in this work, including insufficient quality of model-generated reasoning, consistently weak positive emotion recognition, and the failure to capture distributional variation across human annotators, warrant attention in future efforts to improve LLM emotion understanding through training and alignment. Finally, future work may also explore transferring the dataset and framework to downstream emotion support tasks, such as leveraging the complete inferential chain annotations to support the development and evaluation of cognitive reappraisal-based emotion regulation systems. 

6 Limitations 

While the data collection pipeline was designed and reviewed in close collaboration with experienced psychology researchers to ensure the validity of the annotation schema, several limitations remain. First, data are drawn exclusively from English-speaking participants in the United States and the United Kingdom, meaning that appraisal patterns and emotion vocabulary are inevitably shaped by Anglo-American cultural contexts, and the generalizability of our findings to other languages and 9cultures remains to be validated. Furthermore, while the micro-narratives framework reduces the cognitive burden on participants, it introduces the risk of LLM-generated reconstructions incorporat-ing extraneous details, though we mitigated this through extensive prompt refinement. In addition, participants’ self-selection of narrative events leads to a pronounced imbalance between negative and positive emotion samples, which somewhat limits the coverage of the dataset. Finally, due to the high cost of annotation, the third-person annotation set comprises only 500 narratives with five annotators each, which constrains the precision of distributional estimates for subjective variation.

A.1 Related Work Appraisal Theory and Emotion Modeling Affective computing has long been dominated by discrete emotion categories (e.g., Ekman’s six basic emotions [ 22 ]) and valence-arousal dimensional spaces [ 23 ], which fall short of capturing the richness of real-world emotional experience [ 24 ]. More recently, appraisal theory has gained traction as a more structured alternative: emotions arise not directly from events, but from individuals’ evaluations of situations along multiple dimensions such as goal relevance, controllability, and accountability [ 17 , 18 , 25 ]. Appraisal process models further characterize appraisal as a multi-stage sequential cognitive process [ 26 , 7 ]. Inspired by appraisal process models, our evaluation framework explicitly incorporates the reasoning step into the inferential chain and integrates multiple theoretical accounts of appraisal and emotion into a unified structure, enabling systematic examination of whether LLMs’ emotion-related behaviors align with the predictions of established psychological theories.

Appraisal-Annotated Datasets Several datasets have been constructed to ground emotion an-notation in appraisal theory. Table A1 summarizes their key characteristics in comparison to ours. Specifically, Troiano et al. [ 3] built a dual-perspective appraisal corpus (Crowd-Envent) comprising both author self-reports and reader reconstructions, demonstrating that appraisal dimensions can be reliably predicted from text and improve emotion classification. However, it consists solely of appraisal rating scales without capturing the underlying reasoning process. CovidET-Appraisals [ 27 ]annotated 24 appraisal dimensions with natural language rationales on Reddit posts about Covid-19, though the dataset is limited in scale (241 posts) and covers only a single perspective with 12 single-annotator labels. PEACE-Reviews [ 28 ] provides appraisal ratings and multi-label emotion annotations over post-purchase reviews, yet similarly lacks appraisal reasoning and covers only the third-person perspective. Another line of work introduces appraisal annotations over synthetically generated scenarios, including LLM-generated Chinese emotional dialogues [ 4], model-generated scenarios with appraisal reasoning [ 5], and a forward and backward appraisal-emotion reasoning benchmark based on Prisoner’s Dilemma scenarios [ 29 ]; as these datasets are not grounded in au-thentic human experience, their annotations can only be validated against theoretical predictions rather than reflecting genuine individual appraisal patterns. In contrast, our dataset is grounded in real emotional narratives and, to our knowledge, is the first to provide complete inferential chain annotations from both first-person and third-person perspectives — spanning open-ended appraisal reasoning texts, hierarchical appraisal ratings, and multi-label emotion annotations with polarity — enabling process-level evaluation of LLMs’ emotion understanding. 

Appraisal-Grounded LLM Emotion Understanding Evaluation Several existing studies eval-uating LLMs’ appraisal capabilities have built on the Crowd-Envent dataset, finding that GPT-4 performs as a reasonable appraisal annotator comparable to human readers [ 30 ], but tends to align more closely with third-person observer judgments than first-person self-attributions [ 31 ]; Debnath et al. [ 19 ] further incorporate appraisal as an intermediate representation in dialogue emotion modeling, demonstrating its cross-corpus transferability, while Tak et al. reveal through probing analyses that hi-erarchical appraisal signals are present within LLMs’ internal representations [ 20 ]. Gandhi et al. [ 32 ]extend this line of work to a broader set of models (GPT-4, Claude-3, Gemini-1.5-Pro) in controlled settings, also finding that model judgments can surpass human performance in stereotypical scenarios, though their stimuli are synthetically generated and cover only single emotion labels, limiting ecolog-ical validity. Yeo & Jaidka [ 29 ] and Yongsatianchot et al. [ 33 ] evaluate LLMs’ appraisal reasoning in constrained settings — a Prisoner’s Dilemma paradigm and a psychological stress questionnaire, respectively — using theoretical predictions as the comparison baseline. However, none of the above work accounts for individual differences in emotional understanding. Zhou et al. [ 16 ] address this gap by modeling subjectivity in appraisal judgments as distributions, finding that LLMs fall short in capturing inter-individual appraisal variance. Building on these efforts, our work provides a systematic evaluation spanning the full inferential chain from appraisal reasoning to multi-emotion recognition, while jointly covering first-person emotion understanding and third-person distributional heterogeneity modeling. 

A.2 Data Collection Details Scenario Collection Scenarios were collected using the micro-narratives framework, in which participants respond to a series of sub-questions guided by an LLM, whose responses are then reorganized and minimally reformulated by the LLM into a coherent narrative. The framework aims to reduce the linguistic burden of generating extended text while preserving participants’ own words and voice as closely as possible [ 8]. The sub-questions for scenario collection were: (1) What happened? (2) What were you trying to do? What did you want or need in this situation? (3) How did the situation leave you feeling? (4) Is there anything else that was important to you in this situation? The questions were designed to capture the aspects of the situation most salient to the participant and most likely to have elicited their emotional response, and were finalized through review of pilot study data and discussion with psychology researchers. 

Appraisal Inference and Ratings For each narrative, we collected open-ended inference texts for five core appraisal dimensions — relevance, certainty, congruence, control, and accountability — and subsequently Likert ratings for 22 appraisal items derived through hierarchical expansion of these dimensions. This design draws on Krajuškina et al.[ 25 ], who note that the five core dimensions are included in the vast majority of appraisal theories, capture the broad aspects of situational appraisal, and yield the most stable predictions of affective valence; granular sub-dimensions, in turn, serve as specific instantiations of the broad dimensions, reflecting the particular reasons why a situation may be appraised in a given way along a broad dimension, and can capture affective variation that broad dimension scores alone cannot explain. Accordingly, we use inference texts to cover the core dimensions and complement them with rating scores at the granular level. Core appraisal inference texts were likewise collected using the micro-narratives framework, with one guided question per core dimension. Each question was designed to elicit the annotator’s interpretation 13 of the situation along the corresponding dimension. The definitions of the five dimensions and their associated questions are presented below. 

Relevance : the importance of the situation for the annotator’s salient goals, needs, and motives. 

“When the event happened, how much did it matter to you? Why?” 

Certainty : the clarity of the situation and the predictability of its implications. 

“Did things go as you expected? If not, what was unexpected?” 

Congruence : the extent to which the situation facilitates or obstructs the pursuit of one’s motives. 

“In what ways did this situation help or hurt you?” 

Control : the perceived ability to change the situation or adapt to its effects. 

“Did you feel like you were in control? Why?” 

Accountability : the perceived locus of responsibility for the situation. 

“Who or what did you feel was most responsible for all of this?” 

Appraisal ratings were collected using 22 items derived through hierarchical expansion of the five core dimensions. Each item is presented as a statement, which annotators rated on a 5-point Likert scale based on their perception of the situation. Quality Control To ensure data quality, we conducted multiple rounds of pilot studies prior to the main data collection, iteratively refining the procedure and instructions based on participant feedback in collaboration with psychology experts. For formal data collection, participants were required to reside in the United Kingdom or the United States, with English as both their first and primary language, fluent English proficiency, a Prolific approval rate above 90%, and more than 50 prior submissions on the platform. Case Studies: Appraisal Reasoning 

Example 1 (preferred: human observer; dimension: control) 

Scenario: My favorite team, Michigan, was ranked number 1 and was playing against Duke, who was ranked number 3, on Saturday. It was a big game, and I was really hoping for Michigan to win. I was watching the game, rooting for them, and I even bet on them. Unfortunately, Michigan’s guards didn’t have a good game, and they lost. I felt upset and frustrated after the game. Then, the new rankings came out on Monday, and now Duke is number 1 and Michigan is number 3. 

Question: Did you feel like you were in control? Why? 

1st-person reference: No, I didn’t feel like I was in control because I was just watching the game from home. 

Human observer: I had no control as I was only watching the game and I have no impact on how well they play. 

Claude: No, I didn’t feel in control at all. I could cheer and bet, but I couldn’t influence how Michigan’s guards played. Watching them struggle and losing money on the bet made me feel completely powerless and frustrated. 

Example 2 (preferred: human observer; dimension: certainty) 

Scenario: I accompanied my wife to the doctor to deal with a pain she’s had since giving birth to our son six years ago. We had to go over the experience of that difficult birth with the doctor, and it made both of us relive it and break down in tears. We were trying to get a diagnosis for my wife’s continued pain by giving as clear a description of the cause as possible. We felt emotionally drained but also pleased because we finally got a promising diagnosis after years of trying. It was important to me to ensure my wife had some clarity and hope. 

Question: Did things go as you expected? If not, what was unexpected? 

1st-person reference: Things went pretty much as expected. After 6 years of trying I suppose it was quite surprising to get a diagnosis but I was optimistic. 

Human observer: It went good as we got a promising diagnosis, that is great news. 

Claude: I expected it to be difficult, but I didn’t anticipate how deeply reliving that birth would affect us both. Breaking down in tears caught me off guard. What I didn’t expect was leaving with actual hope—a real diagnosis after years of nothing. That was a relief I hadn’t let myself count on. 

Example 3 (preferred: Claude; dimension: relevance) 

Scenario: Me and my family had booked a trip to Disneyland Paris, flying from Edinburgh to Paris with Air France. We were all set for a fun family holiday, with only two days planned at the theme park. When we got to the airport, we boarded the plane and were just about to take off when the flight was cancelled, completely ruining our trip. I felt very, very angry and heartbroken at the same time. It was especially upsetting for our kids because we had surprised them with the trip that morning, and they were so excited, only to be told they couldn’t go. 

Question: When the event happened, how much did it matter to you? Why? 

1st-person reference: It happened last weekend. It was our first trip there so it mattered a lot! Creating memories with our kids that we couldn’t then do. 

Human observer: It mattered a lot because it was a massive disappointment to my kids. 

Claude: It mattered enormously. We’d planned this special surprise for our kids, and seeing their excitement crushed was devastating. It wasn’t just a holiday—it was a magical moment stolen from them. The anger and heartbreak I felt was overwhelming; I’d never felt so helpless. 

Example 4 (preferred: Claude; dimension: congruence) 

19 Scenario: A friend’s mother passed away this past weekend. Even though it was expected—she was 96 and had been in ill-health and under hospice care for some time—it’s always emotional to lose someone who has been part of your life. I wanted to help and be there for my friend and their family. Unfortunately, my friend had the flu and didn’t want anyone to come over. I didn’t really need anything other than to support my friend, which I did as much as possible from a distance. I suppose it left me feeling ‘OK’. At my age and with my experience, death is becoming common and I know how to deal with it. No, other than we will miss the mother. 

Question In what ways did this situation help or hurt you? 

1st-person reference: The situation didn’t really help or hurt me. 

Human observer: It hurt me as someone who I knew died. 

Claude: It didn’t really hurt me personally. I know how to handle loss at my stage of life. The only frustration was not being able to be there in person for my friend. Supporting from a distance felt limiting, but I managed okay. 

Example 5 (preferred: Claude; dimension: accountability) 

Scenario: It was a very sad time because my friend had to have her dear, precious dog put to sleep. I’ve been giving my friend lots of support and care, and I really wish I could have been able to see her more during this tough time. I’m feeling very sad myself because he was a really lovely, friendly dog and a real miss for both my dear friend and me. You feel low and empty when something like this happens. It’s important to keep positive for my friend, so I’ve been telling her about all the wonderful memories we have and what a great life he had with her. 

Question: Who or what did you feel was most responsible for all of this? 

1st-person reference: No one is responsible as nature and age is sadly what we all face, as he was 14 years old. 

Human observer: It’s just life. 

Claude: Honestly, no one was really responsible—it was simply the heartbreak of losing a beloved pet to old age or illness. Nature takes its course, and that’s the hardest, most unavoidable truth. No blame, just deep sadness.
