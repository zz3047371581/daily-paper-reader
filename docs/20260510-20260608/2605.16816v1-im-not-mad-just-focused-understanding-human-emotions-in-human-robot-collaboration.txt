# “I’m Not Mad, Just Focused”: Understanding Human Emotions in Human-Robot Collaboration 

Seung Chan Hong 1, Dana Kuli´ c1,2 and Leimin Tian 1,2

Abstract —Human-robot collaboration (HRC) can benefit from robots’ abilities to interpret human emotional states. However, current emotion recognition (ER) models in HRC often fall short, particularly due to their reliance on acted datasets and single-modality inputs like facial expressions. We propose a novel vision language model (VLM)-based ER system that leverages contextual understanding to improve emotion interpretation in HRC. We first evaluate the VLM-ER system by assessing its semantic and sentiment similarity with human annotations on an existing HRC dataset. Then, in a user study with a service robot in a collaborative delivery task, we evaluate the effects of modulating the robot’s behaviour based on the user’s emotional state inferred by the VLM-ER system. The results show that the proposed VLM-ER system achieves higher semantic similarity and positive sentiment alignment with human annotations compared to a baseline convolutional neural network-based system. Further, participants in the user study preferred emotion-adaptive robot behaviour facilitated by the VLM-ER system. 

Index Terms —Emotional Robotics, Human-Robot Collabora-tion, Social HRI 

I. INTRODUCTION 

# COLLABORATIVE robots are increasingly integrated in shared spaces with humans [1]. In real-world human-robot collaboration (HRC) scenarios, improving emotion recognition could improve confidence and the trust between the human and the collaborative robots [2]. While emotion recognition (ER) models have been extensively used in social robots [3], less attention has focused on their performance and use in collaborative robots. Current ER models are often trained on acted facial expressions, which are usually exaggerated compared to everyday emotions [4], while natural expressions are sometimes confounded by other facial activities (e.g., speaking) and interaction contexts, limiting their ability to generalise to natural HRC scenarios [5]. This reliance on isolated facial features overlooks findings in psychology, which demonstrate that contextual and body cues are often necessary to accurately discriminate human emotions [6]. II. RELATED WORK 

A. Visual Emotion Recognition in HRC 

Current ER models used by collaborative robots primarily rely on physiological signals, speech-based recognition and facial expression analysis to infer human emotions [10]. Of the existing modalities used for emotion recognition, facial expression recognition (FER) is the more commonly adopted ER approach in HRC as it uses non-intrusive visual information that is representative of human emotions [11]. Standard FER approaches in robotics typically rely on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) trained on large datasets of discrete emotion categories such as AffectNet [12]. For instance, recent work by Venkatesan et al. [13] utilised the DeepFace architecture to achieve a reported 94% average accuracy in classifying natural human emotions. While effective in benchmark settings, these models were reported to have worse performance in recognising subtle and natural expressions that occur within the HRC context [14]. In goal-oriented tasks, for example, a user’s frown might indicate concentration rather than anger, yet standard classifiers often misinterpret this as a negative state [7]. This lack of contextual grounding could limit the utility of traditional FER for informing adaptive robot behaviours. 

B. Contextual Understanding via Vision Language Models 

To address the limitations of isolated facial classification, recent research has looked toward models that can process broader contextual cues. Findings from psychology suggest that emotions arise from an individual’s evaluation of events in relation to their goals and environment [15], highlighting the necessity of incorporating environmental context—such as the state of the task or the robot’s recent actions—into the emotion recognition process. Large Vision Language Models (VLMs) have emerged as a promising tool for this purpose. Unlike supervised learning based CNNs that output fixed labels, VLMs leverage exten-sive pre-training to generate semantic descriptions of scenes, integrating temporal and spatial visual cues across multiple video frames, thus having an enhanced ability to understand context [16]. This has allowed VLMs to outperform classical models at processing and interpreting diverse inputs such as speech, visual cues and text, leading to improved interpretations and emotional inference accuracy [9], [17]. However, their application to affective interpretation and effectiveness in HRC remains underexplored. This work targets this gap by investigating whether the context-aware capabilities of VLMs can provide a more nuanced interpretation of human states during HRC compared to emotion classification. We posit that by moving from discrete labels to semantic scene description, allowing robots to achieve ’visual contextual awareness’ that is better aligned with human observation, potentially enabling more appropriate adaptive responses in collaborative settings. III. S TUDY 1: ER PERFORMANCE OF VLM IN HRC 

A. Methodology 

We investigate how effectively a VLM-ER system can interpret human emotions in HRC and adapt robot behaviour accordingly to improve interaction outcomes in two studies: a comparative evaluation to assess the ER performance of the proposed model on a labelled HRC dataset (Study 1) and an interactive user study in a service robot scenario (Study 2). The procedures for both studies have been reviewed and approved by the Monash University Human Research Ethics Committee (Project ID 47179). 

a) HRC emotion annotation: We annotated videos of human-robot handover from the FACT-HRC dataset [18], [19] for evaluating the performance of ER models in Study 1. These videos depict a Fetch mobile manipulator robot assisting a user in assembling and painting a wooden bird feeder. The robot brought various objects required during this crafting task from a storage area to the user and took objects that the user no longer needed back. The dataset included demographic information of the participants, annotations of each handover episode’s quality, 

Fig. 1: Overview of the participant’s crafting area recorded by the RGB-D camera in the FACT-HRC dataset. video recordings from the robot’s onboard camera, as well as an overview RGB-D camera set up next to the participant’s crafting area, as shown in Figure 1. We manually selected 36 handover episodes recorded from the RGB-D camera from different participants with balanced gender, handover quality, and crafting stage (preparation, assembly, painting) to capture the diverse conditions in HRC. We collected crowdsourced annotations of emotions ex-pressed by the participants in the selected videos via the Prolific platform. Instead of constraining annotators to a fixed set of discrete emotion categories, we collected open-ended, free-text descriptions of observed emotions. Each annotator was given 3 randomly assigned handover videos to annotate with the instruction: “Please describe what emotions the human is expressing with 1–2 sentences.” This approach captures more expressive and nuanced representations of emotional states [20]. We collected a minimum of 3 free-text descriptions per video, with 3 videos having 27 descriptions due to a glitch in the response counter. The annotation task took approximately 10 minutes to complete, and each annotator was paid 1.3 GBP. 

b) ER models: We compared two ER approaches: a CNN baseline model and our proposed VLM. The CNN model consisted of pre-trained DeepFace [21] for facial emotion recognition and YOLOv9c [22] for object detection, i.e., the stacked CNN baseline model as shown in Figure 2. DeepFace was chosen for its established high performance in real-time human emotion recognition. YOLOv9c was selected as a high-performing compact object detector, a choice validated by its competitive accuracy, as the YOLOv9 family, specifically, outperformed the subsequent YOLOv10 models by an average of 2.075% mAP on two benchmarking datasets, also outper-forming the newest YOLOv12 models [23]. The CNN model’s output was a concatenation of predicted emotion(s) and detected object(s). For the object detection stage, the YOLOv9c model was run with a confidence threshold of 0.8 to reduce false positives. The emotion recognition did not employ a confidence threshold, and all predictions from these models were retained for analysis. We combined the facial emotion recognition and object detection CNN models because our qualitative analysis of the human observer annotations showed that the annotators described the emotions in context of the objects in the scene. For the proposed VLM-ER model, we utilised Gemini 2.5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Flash. Although specialised non-VLM architectures (e.g., multimodal Transformers) show HONG et al. : “I’M NOT MAD, JUST FOCUSED”: UNDERSTANDING HUMAN EMOTIONS IN HUMAN-ROBOT COLLABORATION 3

Fig. 2: Stacked CNN baseline using DeepFace for emotion recognition and YOLOv9c for object detection, with outputs concatenated for comparison with human observer outputs. high performance in classification-only tasks, they lack the contextual reasoning depth necessary for interactive social robotics. Our selected models provide state-of-the-art emotional reasoning capabilities on the MME-Emotion benchmark [24], allowing the robot to interpret and act upon complex human affective states more effectively than traditional discriminative baselines. It is important to note the fundamental difference in the output structure between the two approaches. The VLMs are generative models designed to produce descriptive, sentence-based interpretations of a scene. In contrast, the stacked CNN approach is representative of traditional classification-based systems, producing a concatenated list of discrete labels: the dominant emotion predicted by DeepFace and objects detected by YOLOv9c. Therefore, this comparison is designed not as a direct performance benchmark between ER systems, but rather to evaluate which architectural approach, generative description versus discrete classification, produces outputs more semantically and sentimentally aligned with human interpretation of emotions in an HRC context. 

c) Analysis approach: Before embedding, all text re-sponses (human annotations, VLM, and CNN outputs) were preprocessed by removing stop words and applying lemmatisa-tion. All preprocessed text was embedded using the BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 model [25], which produces high-dimensional semantic vector representations. To evaluate ER performance, we calculated the cosine similarity between model output embeddings and averaged annotator label embeddings. Higher cosine similarity indicates stronger semantic alignment with human annotations. Addi-tionally, we computed the difference in compound sentiment scores between each model output and its corresponding human annotation using the VADER sentiment analysis tool [26]. For each video, the stacked CNN output (DeepFace + YOLOv9c), Gemini 2.5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Flash outputs were compared against each human annotation, where their cosine similarity was averaged for the similarity of their video. Mean and standard deviation of cosine similarity and sentiment differences were calculated across all videos. A one-way ANOVA and post-hoc Tukey’s HSD tests were used to evaluate whether Gemini 2.5 Pro, Gemini 2.5 Flash and stacked CNN outputs showed statistically higher alignment with human annotation against one another. To disentangle the effects of semantic and syntactic similarity, we conducted an ablation study with Gemini 2.5 Flash, the VLM used for Study 2. We introduced a condition, the ‘VLM-Classifier‘, designed to mimic the ‘Stacked CNN‘’s label-based output. The VLM was given the following prompt: “Analyze this video. First, choose the single most dominant human emotion from the following list: [happy, sad, angry, neutral, surprise, fear, disgust]. Second, list up to the four most dominant subjects and objects in the scene relevant to the interaction. Format your answer ONLY as: emotion label, object1, object2, object3, object4”. The resulting comma-separated string (e.g., ‘”neutral, person, chair, box”‘) was analysed as follows: Combined (full, unprocessed string); Emotion-Only (first term before the initial comma); and Objects-Only (all terms following the initial comma). This allows for a direct, format-controlled comparison against the Stacked CNN’s combined output and its isolated emotion (DeepFace) and object (YOLOv9c) components. By neutralizing the VLM’s generative sentence structure, we ensure the embedding-based comparison reflects underlying semantic meaning rather than syntactic similarity. All outputs were compared against the lemmatised and stop-word-stripped human annotations using cosine similarity, with a paired t-test to compare Gemini 2.5 Flash against the stacked CNNs.

D. Qualitative Analysis of User Perceptions 

A thematic analysis of exit interviews provided context for our quantitative findings, revealing a clear dissociation between preference and trust. 

a) Preference Driven by Perceived Social Intelligence: 

The strong preference for the EA condition was consistently linked to the robot’s perceived social qualities. Participants described the adaptive apology with anthropomorphic terms like “sincere” and felt it showed “accountability.” One noted that a “more sincere apology made it okay” after a failure. This suggests the adaptive response successfully managed the user’s immediate emotional reaction by framing the interaction in a more positive social light. 

b) Trust Dictated by Functional Reliability: In contrast, comments on trust centred on task performance. Participants directly linked failures to a lack of trustworthiness, stating the robot “wasn’t totally trustworthy because it failed twice” and “felt unreliable.” Comparisons to more “polished and professional” commercial robots sharpened these perceptions. This may suggest that the functional failure itself, not the recovery style, likely drove most of the decrease in trust. 

c) Sceptical Perceptions of Adaptive Behaviour: A mi-nority of participants, particularly those with prior robotics experience, were unconvinced. One found the repeated apology “very disrespectful,” suggesting that for experienced users, simple adaptive behaviours can seem patronising. Another felt the robot was a “proxy to the operator,” indicating its social intelligence was not perceived as authentic. V. D ISCUSSION 

Our investigation into VLM-driven emotion recognition offers preliminary insights into the potential—and the complex-ities—of integrating semantic emotion inference into collabora-tive robots. While our results suggest that VLMs can interpret scenes more similarly to human observers than classification-based methods, the translation of this capability into effective HRC interaction strategies reveals nuanced challenges regarding perspective, trust, and the risks of personalization. 

A. Validating VLMs for Contextual Interpretation 

Study 1 served as a validation of the VLM approach against a classification-based computer vision approach. The significant performance gap between the VLM and the stacked CNN baseline confirms that for external observers, emotion is not merely a facial configuration but a contextual event. The CNN approach, limited to discrete labels and bounding boxes, failed to capture the semantic “story” of the interaction (e.g., “focused on the task”). In contrast, the VLM demonstrated a capability to generate descriptions that aligned semantically and sentimentally with third-person human annotators. This suggests that VLMs are a viable tool for equipping robots with a “third-person” understanding of facial expression, allowing them to interpret the user’s state in the context of the environment and the task, rather than in isolation. 

B. The Gap Between Observation and Experience 

A critical finding emerges when comparing the recognition performance across the two studies. In Study 1, where model outputs were compared against third-person human annotations, the semantic similarity was high ( M = 0 .860 ). However, in Study 2, when compared against the users’ first-person self-reported emotions, this similarity dropped ( M = 0 .693 ). This discrepancy likely highlights the fundamental difference between observed emotion and felt emotion. The VLM, like the human annotators in Study 1, relies entirely on visual cues, body language, facial expressions, and scene context. It does not have access to the user’s internal state. Consequently, while the VLM may be an excellent “observer,” accurately describing how a user appears , it may miss how the user truly feels. Furthermore, this gap may be exacerbated by the timing of data collection; participants reported their emotions after the handover sequence concluded, potentially introducing recall bias or rationalisation of their initial reactive state. This suggests that while VLMs can effectively mimic social observation, they should not be assumed to be perfect empaths of internal user states. 

C. The Complexity of Responding to Failure 

Study 2 introduced a complex interaction chain: a functional error occurred, the user reacted, the VLM interpreted that reaction, and the robot adapted its apology. The strong user preference for the Emotion-Adaptive (EA) condition indicates that, generally, users appreciate the robot’s attempt to acknowledge the social context of the failure. However, the Bayesian analysis revealed that this preference did not translate into preserved trust. The functional failure itself was the dominant factor; no amount of “charming” or empathetic apology could mitigate the loss of trust caused by the robot’s physical incompetence. This suggests a hierarchy of needs in HRC: functional reliability is foundational. Emotion-aware behaviours act as a social lubricant that improves subjective preference and likability, but they cannot compensate for a lack of task capability. 

D. Personalisation: High-Risk, High-Reward 

The qualitative feedback reveals that emotion-based personal-isation is a high-risk, high-reward strategy, as it is possible for the robot to misinterpret the user’s emotion. For the majority of participants with no prior experience with service robots, when the robot misinterpreted their emotion, the adaptive apology was still perceived to be “sincere” and “accountable.” However, a subset of users, particularly those with prior robotics experience, perceived the same behaviour as “disrespectful” or patronising. These failure cases highlight the need for interaction strategies that better take emotion recognition uncertainty into account. Furthermore, our analysis focused on immediate reactions during brief tasks. As perceived sincerity and trust are dynamic, future research should explore how these perceptions evolve over repeated or long-term collaborative engagements. Therefore, while VLM-based adaptation shows promise, it requires careful calibration. An adaptive system must balance social signalling with functional efficiency, ensuring that personalisation attempts do not interfere with the user’s primary goal: completing the task. 

E. Limitations and Future Works  

> •

Controlled Environment: The experiment was conducted in a controlled laboratory setting. This environment lacks the dynamic complexities of a real-world scenario, such as a bustling restaurant with background noise, unpredictable obstacles, and multiple simultaneous interactions.  

> •

Participant Demographics: Our participants were primar-ily recruited from a university population, which may not be representative of a typical restaurant’s demographic in terms of age, technical familiarity, and cultural background. Perceptions of robot behaviour can vary significantly across different groups.  

> •

Single VLM Architecture: While we compared two versions of Gemini, we did not test it against other state-of-the-art VLMs. The performance and nature of adaptive responses could differ with other models.  

> •

Limited Context and Task: The collaborative task was simple, with a scripted failure event identical for all participants, likely limiting emotional range.  

> •

Short-Term Interaction: This study examined immediate reactions during brief interactions. However, perceptions of trust and sincerity may evolve over repeated or long-term tasks, necessitating future longitudinal investigation.  

> •

Latency: Measured latency inherent in cloud-based infer-ence was 6.82 s. While unsuitable for reflexive reactions, this remains feasible for inferencing during transition periods, where the behaviour is updated before the 8 IEEE ROBOTICS AND AUTOMATION LETTERS. > •

Different Adaptation Strategies: Future iterations could integrate online affect recognition to proactively adjust behavior or request help before failures occur. Beyond failure prevention, recovery could be improved by explain-ing failure causes or proposing alternative actions for user selection.  

> •

Lack of Performance Metrics: We did not measure objective task performance metrics, such as completion time or error rates in the candy bag assembly. This limited our ability to assess how the robot’s behaviour affected task efficiency. VI. C ONCLUSION 

This work presents a preliminary exploration into using Vision Language Models for emotion-aware human-robot collaboration. We demonstrated that VLMs significantly out-perform classification-based CNN approaches in aligning with human observer interpretations of emotional scenes. However, our user study highlights that being a good observer does not automatically restore trust after a failure in human-robot collaboration. While users preferred the adaptive social behaviour, their trust remained tethered to the robot’s reliability. Future work may focus on bridging the gap between observed and felt emotions, such as by integrating longer-term interaction history, to create robots that are not just socially reactive, but truly collaborative partners.
