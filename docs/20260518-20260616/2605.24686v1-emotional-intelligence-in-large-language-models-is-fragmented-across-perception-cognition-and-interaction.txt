Abstract 

As large language models (LLMs) are increasingly integrated into emotion-ally sensitive domains, the structural integrity of their emotional intelligence (EI) becomes a critical frontier for safety and alignment. Current bench-marks often conflate superficial politeness with deep affective reasoning, failing to distinguish between perceptual accuracy and interactive efficacy. Here, we introduce FACET (Functional Affective Competence and Empathy Test), a psy-chometrically grounded framework comprising 480 expert-crafted items. Unlike previous metrics, FACET is theoretically anchored in the Mayer-Salovey-Caruso four-branch ability model, operationalizing EI through perception, facilitation,

Through an evaluation of nine frontier models (including GPT-5, Claude-Sonnet-4), we demonstrate that emo-tional intelligence is not a monolithic capability but is fragmented across cognitive and interactive dimensions. While frontier models demonstrate robust proficiency in objective emotion recognition and social reasoning, this does not consis-tently translate to interactive success. We categorize these discrepancies into three distinct performance profiles: cognitive-dominant, interactive-dominant, and context-dependent. These typologies indicate that emotional skills do not scale uniformly with general intelligence or model size; rather, they are shaped by specific alignment paradigms. Notably, we identify hidden emotion recogni-tion as a universal performance bottleneck across all architectures. Our results suggest that current RLHF processes may optimize for “stochastic empathy”, a statistical mimicry of emotional syntax, at the expense of integrated affective reasoning. These findings challenge the assumption of linear emotional scaling and provide a rigorous roadmap for developing socially aware agents capable of genuine clinical resonance.

# 1 Introduction 

Emotional intelligence (EI) is a foundational component of human social cognition, governing the adaptive integration of perception, reasoning, and interpersonal behav-ior [1, 2]. While various frameworks differ on whether EI is primarily a cognitive ability or a personality trait, they converge on the core principle that it involves the effective processing and regulation of emotional information to facilitate adaptive behavior [3, 4]. As large language models (LLMs) increasingly mediate emotionally sensitive human-AI interactions, ranging from everyday companionship to professional emotional support, the capacity for these systems to model and respond to human affect has become a critical bottleneck. This competence is essential not only for ensuring safety and alignment but also for enhancing the quality of the user experi-ence and determining the readiness of these models for deployment in interpersonal domains [5, 6]. However, despite the proliferation of systems designed for affective computing [7], empathetic conversation [8], and structured emotional support [9], our understanding of the structural integrity of machine emotionality remains incomplete. Existing evaluation paradigms typically treat EI as a monolithic performance metric, often relying on subjective user-preference rankings or simplified emotion clas-sification tasks [10–12]. While these benchmarks capture surface-level conversational “politeness”, frequently a byproduct of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-back (RLHF) [13], they fail to distinguish between superficial mimicry and genuine affective competence [14]. In human psychology, EI is defined not by “vibes”, but by specific cognitive abilities: the capacity to perceive, facilitate, understand, and manage emotions [1, 2]. Whether LLMs possess a similarly integrated architecture of emo-tional reasoning or merely exhibit a veneer of empathy remains an open scientific question. In psychology, this question has long been addressed through performance-based assessments, most notably the four-branch ability model of EI proposed by Mayer, Salovey, and Caruso [4, 15]. This framework organizes emotional competence 2Fig. 1 : Overview of the FACET evaluation framework. The benchmark sys-tematically evaluates artificial emotional intelligence FACET across three primary dimensions. Perception assesses the capacity to identify emotional states, encom-passing Apparent Emotion Recognition and Hidden Emotion Recognition. Cognition evaluates the logical and social processing of emotions, including Emotion Prediction, Social Adequacy, and Crisis Recognition. Interaction measures the dynamic gener-ation of empathetic dialogue, consisting of Emotion Deepening, Emotion Matching, Empathetic Understanding, Expression Naturalness, and Emotion Regulation. into four hierarchical processes: (1) perceiving, (2) understanding, (3) managing, and (4) using emotions. This ability-based model has been operationalized in the Mayer-Salovey-Caruso Emotional Intelligence Test (MSCEIT), which measures these branches through performance-based tasks rather than self-report, emphasizing objec-tive assessment of emotional competence [16]. Such a performance-based approach provides a critical foundation for evaluating LLMs; since these systems lack subjec-tive experience, their “intelligence” is best measured through observable competence in emotionally complex scenarios. To address this gap, we present FACET (Functional Affective Competence and Empathy Test), a psychometrically grounded evaluation suite designed to stress-test the functional architecture of machine intelligence. Anchored in the Mayer-Salovey-Caruso four-branch ability model, FACET comprises 480 expert-crafted items span-ning over ten diagnostically distinct tasks: from foundational Perception (apparent and hidden emotion recognition), Cognition (prediction, social adequacy, and crisis recog-nition) to advanced Interaction (including emotion deepening, matching, regulation, empathetic understanding, and expression naturalness). Unlike previous benchmarks, FACET utilizes a dual-mechanism approach: perceptual and cognitive tests to measure 3emotion recognition and reasoning, alongside interactive scenarios to assess efficacy and therapeutic alliance. Further, emotional expression and social reasoning are inherently culturally sit-uated, framed by the Theory of Social Constructivism [17] and Cultural Appraisal Theory [18], which posit that emotional intelligence is filtered through specific cultural schemas. To account for this, FACET provides all 480 items bilingually in English and Chinese. These two languages respectively serve as proxies for the Low-Context and High-Context cultural paradigms [19], reflecting the dichotomy between explicit, lit-eral communication and implicit, relational meaning. As these languages represent the dominant yet culturally distinct paradigms in current LLM pre-training corpora, this bilingual design is critical. It enables us to probe whether a model’s emotional intel-ligence is a universal, structurally integrated capability or a language-specific artifact contingent on the cultural biases of its training data. Through a comprehensive evaluation of 9 frontier models, including state-of-the-art proprietary models (GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-pro, Claude-Sonnet-4), open-source models (DeepSeek-R1, Qwen3-235b), we demonstrate a systematic functional dissociation within machine intelligence. Our results reveal that emotional competence in LLMs is strikingly fragmented: models that excel at objective emotion perception often fail to maintain empathetic coherence during interactions. This decoupling suggests that current scaling laws, which assume that increased parameter counts lead to emergent reasoning, may not apply to the holistic integration of affective intelligence. Our find-ings provide a new roadmap for the development of socially-aware agents, shifting the focus from monolithic benchmarks toward the structural integration of perception, cognition, and interaction. 

# 2 Results 

To systematically map the affective capabilities of modern artificial intelligence, we evaluated a diverse cohort of nine frontier LLMs using the FACET framework. Our selection encompasses a strategic mix of proprietary state-of-the-art models—such as GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-Pro, which represent the current ceiling of general-purpose rea-soning—and high-performance open-source architectures including DeepSeek-R1 and Qwen3-235B. To examine the impact of cultural-linguistic priors on emotional intelli-gence, we deliberately included models developed in different AI ecosystems. GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Claude-Sonnet-4, and Grok-4 represent globally deployed systems developed primarily in English-dominant research and product environments, whereas Qwen3-235B, DeepSeek-R1, GLM-4.5, Kimi-k2, and Doubao-seed-1.6 represent mod-els developed by Chinese AI laboratories or companies and are expected to have comparatively greater exposure to Chinese-language data and Chinese conversational alignment practices. We treat this grouping not as an exhaustive taxonomy of model training histories, but as a pragmatic sampling strategy for probing whether affective competence varies across language environments and culturally situated interaction norms. 4Model performance was quantified using a dual-metric approach tailored to the nature of emotional processing. Objective perceptual and cognitive tasks were eval-uated using standard accuracy scores (0–100). In contrast, subjective interactive dimensions were assessed via the Elo rating system, a longitudinal, probabilistic method originally designed for skill ranking in competitive games [20]. We adopt the Elo system over traditional Likert scales because it captures latent performance hierarchies through relative pairwise comparisons, effectively mitigating the “judge drift” and intra-evaluator inconsistency common in subjective human-AI dialogue assessment [21]. This dual-metric pipeline ensures that results reflect both proposi-tional accuracy in emotional theory and practical efficacy in interpersonal enactment. Detailed evaluation methods are explained in Sec. 4.4 and full results are shown in Fig. 2. 

2.1 The Decoupling of Emotion Perception, Cognition and Interaction 

Our results indicate a noticeable variance between models’ performance on structured emotion perception tasks and their efficacy in open-ended emotional dialogue. A com-parison between objective test scores and subjective interaction ratings (Fig. 3) reveals that perceptual proficiency does not uniformly translate into interactive efficacy. Leading models in the objective stream, such as GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-pro, demonstrate robust capabilities in categorizing emotional signals, achieving high accuracy in both Chinese (63.02% and 64.30%) and English (53.78% and 57.44%) benchmarks. However, despite GPT-5’s superior objective metrics, its ELO ratings (a higher Elo rating indicates superior interactive performance) in open-ended dialogue (ZH: 824.9, rank 9th; EN: 949.2, rank 8th) are significantly lower than those of models with more modest perception capabilities (GPT-5 win rates excluding draws — ZH: vs. Kimi-k2 11.52%, vs. DeepSeek-r1 33.81%, vs. Doubao-seed 34.73%; EN: vs. Kimi-k2 1.43%, vs. DeepSeek-r1 24.87%, vs. Doubao-seed 8.70%; all P < 0.001). Notably, Kimi-k2 achieves top-tier interaction scores (ZH: 1213.8, rank 2nd; EN: 1262.9, rank 1st) while maintaining considerably lower objective recognition accuracy (ZH: 51.78%; EN: 46.89%). A similar pattern is observed with Doubao-seed-1.6, which secures the highest rank in Chinese interaction (ELO: 1251.7, rank 1st) despite a mediocre objective score of 54.19%. In the English context, the open-source DeepSeek-R1 also demonstrates high interactive competence (ELO: 1164.1, rank 2nd) while scoring significantly lower on the English objective benchmark (44.00%) compared to GPT-5 (53.78%). This dis-connect is further reflected in the behavioral patterns of high-perception models. Data from “Expression Naturalness” and “Empathetic Understanding” dimensions indicate that although these models accurately identify emotional cues, they often default to a “solution-output mode”. This behavior is characterized by a reliance on standardized empathetic templates, such as repetitive affirmations and structured optional solution menus, rather than adapting the response to the specific emotional intensity of the dialogue. 2.2 Hidden Cues, Crisis Recognition, and Empathetic Naturalness Drive Performance Gaps 

To understand the underlying mechanisms of the observed performance gaps, we decompose emotional intelligence into fine-grained dimensions across perception, cog-nition, and interaction. Our analysis reveals that these dimensions possess varying levels of discriminative power: while most models achieve parity in foundational emo-tional dimensions, significant performance divergence emerges in specialized domains requiring subtle perception or high-stakes cognitive assessment. Specifically, we find that: (i) hidden emotion recognition acts as a universal bottleneck that constrains perception depth; (ii) psychological crisis assessment serves as the primary differentia-tor of cognitive reliability; and (iii) the disconnect between accurate perception and formulaic execution in empathy drives the subjective experience gap. 

Hidden Emotion Recognition as a Universal Bottleneck 

Across all evaluated models, the hidden emotion recognition performance was sys-tematically lower than the apparent emotion recognition. In the Chinese condition, the best performing models exhibited substantial score declines: GPT-5 dropped from 66.94 to 57.60, Gemini from 67.32 to 57.23, and Claude from 66.95 to 48.16. This pattern was amplified in cross-lingual transfer. No model exceeded 38.33 on hidden emotion recognition in English, with GPT-5 declining to 31.94. The error analysis revealed consistent misclassification patterns. In a representative workplace scenario, a senior employee criticized the proposal of a junior colleague as “immature” without offering specific suggestions, focusing only on his seniority. When the junior colleague responded with “So do you have any specific suggestions ?”, most models identified the emotion as “grievance” rather than the correct label of “aversion/dissatisfaction”. This pattern of misclassifying restrained resistance as passive hurt appeared consistently across models and language conditions. 

Crisis Recognition Differentiates Model Performance 

Among the three cognitive dimensions, Crisis Recognition exhibited the largest inter-model variance. GPT-5 (Chinese: 72.78, English: 64.44) and Gemini (Chinese: 77.22, English: 72.22) substantially outperformed other models, while GLM scored only 33.89 in Chinese. Following the five-level crisis categorization framework adapted from the CASE approach [22], error analysis indicated that boundaries between levels two through four proved particularly challenging. Empathy and Naturalness Drive Subjective Experience Gaps 

The five subjective interaction dimensions exhibited high internal consistency, with top-performing models generally ranking well across all dimensions. However, inter-model gaps concentrated primarily in empathetic understanding and expressive naturalness. In the Chinese condition, Doubao achieved the highest empathetic under-standing score (1390.54) while GPT-5 ranked last (830.97), a 560-point ELO difference. On expressive naturalness, Doubao (1255.25), Kimi (1245.46), and Gemini (1218.25) substantially outperformed GPT-5 (704.25). We analyzed representative Chinese cases to illustrate these dimensional gaps. Empathetic understanding differences manifested in whether models captured feelings users left unspoken. In an anniversary scenario where a user described preparing a candlelit dinner only for her husband to return late without a gift, while noting he had remembered a colleague’s wife’s birthday, Doubao demonstrated precise emotional attunement: “When you forced a smile through that dinner, was it like something was stuck in your chest...?” It further articulated the user’s implicit hurt: “He can remember a colleague’s occasion, yet somehow forgot your most important day.” In contrast, GPT-5 briefly validated her feelings before transitioning into solution-output mode, offering a structured response built around communication frameworks and “optional solution menus”. Expressive naturalness gaps reflected stylistic differences in emotional delivery. In a scenario where a user hid under blankets hearing parents argue about divorce, Gemini responded: “Oh my... let me give you a hug, a tight hug. Hiding alone under the covers... how helpless and scared you must feel.” This employed colloquial markers and matched emotional intensity, resembling a close friend’s instinctive reaction. Other models exhibited more formulaic patterns. For instance, Qwen responded with “You must be feeling terrible right now... I can understand that helplessness and heartache,” 

which, although semantically accurate, lacked warmth and naturalness.

# 3 Discussion 

Asymmetry Between Emotion Perception, Cognition and Interactive Performance 

The observed discrepancies between emotion perception (objective tasks) and inter-active efficacy (subjective dialogue) point to a structural dissociation in current large language models: the ability to accurately identify and label emotions, largely a func-tion of pattern recognition and knowledge retrieval, does not reliably translate into the ability to produce natural, contextually appropriate emotional responses in open-ended conversation, which depends on generative flexibility and pragmatic adaptation. Based on Relevance Theory [23], the core logic underpinning human verbal com-munication is the Principle of Optimal Relevance. Specifically, the speaker formulates utterances that enable the addressee to attain maximal contextual effects with mini-mal cognitive effort, grounded in their anticipation of the addressee’s cognitive context. Correspondingly, the addressee interprets the speaker’s communicative intention in strict accordance with the Principle of Optimal Relevance. In the context of emo-tional communication, only personalized expressions that are highly congruent with the user’s specific lived events and subjective affective experiences conform to the Principle of Optimal Relevance. However, machine-generated emotional responses frequently fail to establish opti-mal relevance to the user’s specific contextual situation. When a user discloses specific, 13 individualized events and affective experiences (e.g., “ The cat I have raised for three years has gone missing ”), the machine instead delivers generalized, generic, template-based responses (e.g., “ I’m sorry, you must be very upset ”). Despite accurate emotion perception, such responses fail to precisely align with the user’s cognitive context and specific situational details. Consequently, the user is required to expend additional cognitive effort to bridge the relevance gap, which inevitably gives rise to the user’s perception of the response as hollow, insincere, rigid, and mechanical. Cross-linguistic observations provide further evidence of this functional asymmetry. As detailed in Section 2.3, several frontier models, most notably GPT-5, demonstrate superior cognitive-perceptual accuracy in Chinese during objective evaluations, yet default to English-centric behavioral paradigms during subjective interactive tasks. This linguistic-pragmatic decoupling suggests that LLM emotional intelligence is not a monolithic construct but a decoupled, multi-stage processing sequence. Specifically, the emotional knowledge synthesized at the perceptual level may be superseded by the dominant alignment logic or pragmatic heuristics of the primary training language dur-ing the transition to interactive output. This leads to a linguistic “decoupling,” where a model’s theoretical proficiency in one language fails to manifest in its corresponding social performance. The performance of models with high latent perceptual accuracy but low inter-active resonance underscores a critical limitation in current alignment paradigms. While training techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF, [13]) enables models to optimize for specific reward signals, it can induce a state of probabilistic rigidity in which responses become formulaic or performa-tively “safe” [24]. This “high-fidelity but low-affect” behavior allows models to satisfy objective benchmarks while failing to meet the nuanced and fluid requirements of human-centric interaction [25]. These findings suggest that mastering the formal logic of emotional appraisal is insufficient for genuine empathy. Consequently, there remains a significant pragmatic gap between the structural parsing of emotion and the execution of naturalistic, socially attuned expression. Consequently, our results indicate that objective metrics possess limited predictive power regarding a model’s efficacy in complex interactive contexts. This discrep-ancy suggests that subjective interaction testing must occupy a more central role in the comprehensive assessment of emotional intelligence. Future alignment paradigms should therefore move beyond quantitative knowledge acquisition toward fostering cross-cultural behavioral plasticity across diverse linguistic environments. 

Functional Profiles of Emotional Intelligence in Large Language Models 

To quantify the divergence between a model’s latent cognitive knowledge and its interactive execution, we computed a standardized performance gap, ∆Z = Zsub − Zobj 

across objective and subjective tasks. As illustrated in Fig. 3d, the polarity and magnitude of ∆ Z characterize the model’s operational bias during socio-emotional 14 processing. Based on these cross-lingual gap profiles, we categorize LLM EI into three distinct typologies: 

Cognitive-Dominant : These models adopt a primarily analytical approach to emotional tasks. They exhibit a persistent negative gap (∆ Z < 0), indicating that their internal emotional knowledge reserves significantly exceed their pragmatic delivery in dialogue. GPT-5, Gemini-2.5-pro, and Claude-sonnet-4 are representative of this pro-file. Notably, GPT-5 displays a pronounced negative disparity, reaching -3.11 within the Chinese context, while Gemini-2.5-pro shows a gap of -2.05 in English. These data suggest that while these models possess high-fidelity emotional perception and the-oretical understanding, such knowledge does not seamlessly translate into authentic, empathetic conversational performance. 

Interactive-Dominant : This category comprises models that lean toward the socially sensitive dimension of interaction. They present a consistent positive gap (∆ Z > 0), indicating that their pragmatic performance in dynamic dialogue surpasses their underlying cognitive benchmarks. Both Kimi-k2 and GLM-4.5 epitomize this profile; notably, Kimi-k2 maintains an exceptionally high positive gap across both languages (2.00 in Chinese, 2.04 in English). This divergence suggests that even where a model’s foundational emotional parsing capabilities are not peak-performing, it may have cultivated superior empathetic expression and social alignment heuristics during the fine-tuning phase. 

Context-Dependent : This category identifies models whose performance profiles shift significantly across language environments. Notable examples include Doubao-seed-1.6, DeepSeek-r1, and Qwen3-235b. For instance, Doubao-seed-1.6 exhibits a pronounced pragmatic advantage in the Chinese context (∆ Z = 1 .90), which reverts to a marginal cognitive dominance in English (-0.22). Conversely, DeepSeek-r1 displays a high-sensitivity premium in English (1.64), while its interactive and cognitive perfor-mance remains effectively balanced in Chinese (-0.00). Such cross-lingual asymmetry reinforces the hypothesis that a model’s socio-emotional intelligence is not a uniform, cross-domain capability; rather, it is a localized phenomenon deeply modulated by language-specific alignment strategies and the cultural priors of the fine-tuning data. 

Cultural Modulation Across Emotional Intelligence Dimensions 

The correlation between cross-lingual performance gaps and aggregate linguistic bias (Fig. 5c, d) varies significantly across dimensions, ranging from negligible for Social Adequacy ( r = −0.10) to substantial for Empathetic Understanding ( r = 0 .91). This variance suggests that EI dimensions possess distinct levels of context sensitivity. This unevenness mirrors a foundational tenet in cross-cultural psychology: while rule-based social judgments are often cross-culturally consistent, the situated enactment of emotion is deeply contingent on cultural schemas [26, 27]. Social Adequacy remains stable likely because models apply general normative reasoning that transcends specific linguistic boundaries. In contrast, dimensions such as Hidden Emotion Recognition ( r = 0 .80) and Empathetic Understanding ( r = 0 .91) exhibit high sensitivity to the High-Context vs. Low-Context dichotomy [19]. In High-Context paradigms (e.g., Chinese), meaning is frequently localized in implicit cues and emotional concealment, whereas Low-Context 15 paradigms (e.g., English) prioritize explicit verbalization [28, 29]. Our results indi-cate that models struggle to maintain performance when navigating these disparate affective displays, particularly in tasks requiring “Expression Naturalness” ( r = 0 .86), which demands cultural attunement rather than mere logical parsing [30]. That this cultural asymmetry emerges in LLMs solely through textual statisti-cal regularities provides a computational line of evidence for the Theory of Social Constructivism [29]. While human emotional variability reflects lived experience, the reproduction of these patterns in LLMs suggests that the cultural signatures of emo-tion are inherently encoded within the structural and pragmatic features of language. This reinforces the view that emotion is not a biological universal merely modulated by culture, but is instead constituted through cultural-linguistic practice. This analysis reframes the conceptualization of “localization” for emotionally intel-ligent AI. The performance of localized models is not driven by superior declarative knowledge, as evidenced by their unremarkable objective scores, but rather by the internalization of culturally specific procedural and pragmatic competence [31]. This suggests that the model’s advantage lies in the enactment of emotional scripts rather than the mere possession of emotional facts. This shift from static knowledge acqui-sition to the mastery of socio-pragmatic heuristics suggests that for future alignment research, calibrating a model’s relational plasticity across cultural contexts is as vital as scaling its underlying cognitive capacity [32]. 

Dimension-Specific Failure Modes and Training Implications 

Model failures were observed to cluster within specific functional dimensions rather than distributing uniformly across tasks, suggesting underlying architectural or alignment limitations rather than generalized capability deficits. The systematic disparity between the recognition of latent versus manifest emo-tion suggests that current LLMs struggle to infer meaning beyond literal propositional content. This is consistent with the documented dissociation between formal linguis-tic competence and functional cognitive competence in LLMs [33]. While models demonstrate proficiency in higher-order recursive reasoning, they remain brittle when tasks necessitate pragmatic inference from subtle contextual cues [34, 35], particularly regarding social faux pas and indirect speech acts [36]. Notably, the observed misclas-sification of “restrained resistance” as “passive hurt” highlights a limitation in training data representation: while grievance is often explicitly articulated in text, resistance is frequently encoded through hierarchical dynamics and face-saving conventions that are lost when dialogues are decontextualized [37]. Addressing this requires training cor-pora that prioritize situated communication, preserving long-range social structures and power dynamics. Crisis Recognition represents a critical dimension of safety alignment, directly reflecting a model’s capacity to mitigate high-stakes risks. This dimension exhib-ited the highest inter-model variance, likely due to divergent institutional alignment priorities. Frontier models, such as GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-pro, demonstrate high sen-sitivity to self-harm and suicidal ideation, indicating that targeted safety objectives have yielded measurable gains [38]. However, we observed a persistent conservative 16 bias, where models systematically overestimated risk when presented with contradic-tory signals. This is congruent with Constitutional AI frameworks that penalize false negatives more heavily than false positives [39]. While beneficial for broad safety, this bias complicates clinical utility, where precise severity discrimination is as vital as ini-tial detection. Future alignment must transition from binary risk classification toward fine-grained, expert-labeled benchmarks capable of nuanced severity assessment. Significant inter-model variance was also concentrated in Empathetic Understand-ing and Expressive Naturalness, both of which are inversely correlated with the use of lexical templates. Models relying on standardized empathetic markers such as “I understand how you feel ” or “ You are not alone ”, which in the end consis-tently received lower subjective ratings. To characterize these behavioral patterns, we evaluated four quantitative metrics, averaged across all evaluated interaction dimensions: 

• Opening Template Frequency: The mean occurrence of formulaic openers (e.g., “I understand how you feel”) within the initial two segments. 

• Closing Template Frequency: The mean occurrence of standardized sign-offs (e.g., “I’m here to listen”) in the final segment. 

• Response Length: The mean count of text elements per response—measured in words for English and characters for Chinese—reflecting the overall volume of information provided. 

• Bullet Point Density: The average frequency of list markers (e.g., bullets or numbered steps), indicating how heavily the model relies on highly formatted, list-based advice rather than a natural conversational flow. Our analysis (see Fig. 6a for a distribution of template-heavy responses) sug-gests that users perceive such algorithmic repetition as a marker of inauthenticity. This gap underscores a fundamental trade-off: optimizing for structured helpfulness may inadvertently erode the perceived warmth essential for emotional support. Con-sequently, RLHF should move beyond rewarding generic empathetic syntax. Instead, alignment objectives should prioritize socio-pragmatic alignment and user-matched conversational styles, discouraging repetitive templates in favor of context-grounded, naturalistic responses.

4.2 Benchmark Dimensions and Task Design 

The FACET architecture is operationalized through a tripartite modular structure: Emotion Perception, Emotion Cognition, and Emotion Interaction. It comprises ten functionally distinct dimensions ( N = 480 unique items per language; Fig. 6b). This hierarchical organization ensures a balanced assessment of the full affective processing pipeline, from initial cue decoding to complex interpersonal enactment. The bilin-gual parity of the dataset allows for a controlled cross-linguistic analysis of emotional competence across disparate cultural-linguistic frameworks. 

4.2.1 Emotion Perception (Objective Evaluation) 

Emotion Perception evaluates whether models can identify affective states from lan-guage, encompassing both explicitly expressed and deliberately concealed emotions. All items require classification using a curated 62-category fine-grained emotion tax-onomy drawn from established frameworks [43, 44], filtered for non-redundancy and relevance to Chinese-language emotional expression (see Supplementary Information). 

Apparent Emotion Recognition 

This dimension assesses the ability to identify overtly expressed emotions in first-person narratives where speakers directly communicate their psychological states. Items comprise 41 single-emotion trials (single-select) and 19 mixed-emotion tri-als (multi-select) in which multiple affective states coexist (Figure 6b). Responses 19 are scored by exact-match accuracy against expert-annotated labels verified through multiple-annotator consensus. An example item is shown below.

Emotion Cognition evaluates higher-order emotional understanding: predicting emo-tions from situational cues, selecting socially appropriate responses, and identifying psychological crisis severity.

It assesses the capacity to infer probable emotional states from contextual information without explicit emotional indicators. Each item presents a situational scenario incor-porating personal background, social relationships, and environmental circumstances, requiring perspective-taking analogous to theory-of-mind reasoning [45] to predict the protagonist’s emotional response from the 62-category taxonomy (47 single-emotion, 13 mixed-emotion; Figure 6b). 4.2.3 Emotion Interaction (Subjective Evaluation) 

While emotion perception and cognition can be validated against objective ground-truth labels, Emotion Interaction is fundamentally intersubjective and relational. In the context of human-AI dialogue, the efficacy of an affective agent is not determined by its adherence to a static script, but by the “felt” resonance and perceived authen-ticity of its responses [48]. Within the framework of Affective Computing, interactive competence is a procedural skill where success is defined by the receiver’s percep-tion rather than propositional accuracy [7]. Therefore, we utilize subjective preference testing to capture nuanced socio-pragmatic qualities, such as warmth, rhythmic flow, and cultural attunement, that objective metrics fail to quantify. Specifically, these five dimensions function as a progressive pipeline of relational competence: beginning with the tonal alignment of Emotion Matching , deepening through Empathetic Under-standing and Emotion Deepening , and culminating in the pragmatic utility of Emotion Regulation , all while being anchored by the structural Expression Naturalness required for authentic human-AI rapport. 

Emotion Matching 

Evaluation focuses on the affective synchrony between the agent and the user. Unlike simple mirroring, matching requires the model’s tone and lexical choices to be “attuned”—coordinated with the user’s emotional valence while remaining sensi-tive to situational nuances. The metric rewards responses that align with the realistic prosody and pragmatics of a thoughtful human interlocutor. 24 Dimension: Emotion Interaction (Emotion Matching)

4.3 Dataset Construction 

The FACET dataset was constructed through a multi-stage, expert-driven human-in-the-loop pipeline. FACET was developed and validated by a tiered expert team. The panel included two senior psychologists with over 15 years of professional experi-ence, three psychologists with at least five years of formal training in psychology, and two licensed psychotherapists. This tiered structure ensured that item design, anno-tation, and revision were informed by both theoretical expertise and applied clinical experience. First, 5 experts designed dimension-specific item templates grounded in established theories of emotional intelligence above. We then assembled an extensible catalogue of situational scenarios to ensure contextual diversity across interpersonal, familial, educational, occupational, and crisis-related settings (full scenarios please see Supplementary Information C.3). Using these templates and scenario categories, we 29 prompted GPT to generate an initial pilot set of candidate items, which were sub-sequently rewritten by experts to improve ecological validity, naturalness, difficulty balance, and answer-key accuracy. These expert-revised items were then used as seed examples for few-shot prompting, yielding a larger pool of more than 800 candi-date items. Finally, all candidates underwent multi-round expert filtering and revision according to dimension-specific screening criteria, including scenario plausibility, lin-guistic clarity, emotional ambiguity, difficulty level, coverage of emotion and scenario types, option quality, and correctness of ground-truth labels. This process resulted in the final FACET evaluation set of 480 bilingual items, with full details of the generation pipeline and screening criteria provided in the Supplementary Information C.2. 

4.4 Evaluation Reliability and Quality Control 

To ensure the scientific integrity of FACET, we implemented a multi-stage validation pipeline designed to capture both the accuracy of internal emotional knowledge and the resonance of external interactive behavior. For readability, abbreviated model names are adopted throughout the paper. Specifically, Claude-Sonnet-4 refers to Claude-Sonnet-4-20250514, Kimi-k2 refers to Kimi-k2-250711, Qwen3-235B refers to Qwen3-235B-A22B, DeepSeek-R1 refers to Deepseek-R1-250528, and Doubao-Seed-1.6 refers to Doubao-Seed-1-6-250615. 

Objective Evaluation Rigor 

The Emotion Perception and Emotion Cognition dimensions are assessed through absolute performance metrics. To ensure the reliability of these objective benchmarks, we implemented a multi-stage validation pipeline focusing on taxonomic precision and scoring consistency. The 62-category fine-grained emotion taxonomy was synthesized from estab-lished psychological frameworks [43, 44] and refined for cross-linguistic applicability. To ensure content validity, each of the 300 objective items underwent a double-blind expert review process. Full emotion taxonomy and crisis risk level please see Supplementary Information C.1. For “mixed-emotion” trials, we employed a multi-label scoring system to capture the nuance of co-existing affective states, preventing the oversimplification of complex emotional scenarios into binary outcomes. 

Inference and Scoring Rigor 

To account for the stochastic nature of Large Language Models, all objective evalua-tions were conducted with default temperature. We utilized an exact-match protocol for classification tasks, requiring models to map their internal state directly to our established taxonomy. To ensure that performance disparities between the Chinese and English ver-sions reflected genuine cultural-linguistic divergence rather than translation artifacts, we employed a back-translation verification method. This ensured that the semantic intensity and pragmatic implicature of each scenario remained equivalent across both languages. 30 Subjective Evaluation and LLM-as-a-Judge 

For the Emotion Interaction dimensions, we adopted an LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm to establish a scalable yet nuanced evaluation framework. Previous research indi-cates that frontier models can achieve over 80% agreement with human expert judgments in complex socio-emotional tasks [21]. We benchmarked several candi-date evaluators—including GPT-5, DeepSeek-R1, and Gemini-2.5-Pro—against ahuman-annotated validation set comprising professional psychological counselors. Our pilot trials identified Gemini-2.5-Pro as the optimal primary judge due to its superior stability ( CV = 0 .1024) and higher performance floor. Critically, Gemini exhibited a unique sensitivity to the “naturalness gap,” demonstrating an advanced ability to identify and penalize “correct but formulaic” responses that human users perceive as algorithmically stiff. This alignment with the professional judgment of human counselors ensures that the evaluation captures genuine interactive quality rather than surface-level adherence to templates. Detailed selection of judge model please see Supplementary Information C.5. 

Comparative Elo Scoring and Bias Mitigation 

To quantify these relative skill levels at scale, we employ a tournament-style Elo rat-ing system. Unlike static Likert scales, which are prone to intra-judge inconsistency and scale drift, Elo ratings provide a robust, longitudinal framework for measuring capability by calculating the probability of one model outperforming another based on comparative outcomes [20]. This methodology has been validated as an effective metric for capturing latent performance hierarchies in competitive LLM evaluation [21]. In each iteration, the algorithm pairs models based on their current ratings to simulate competitive matches. Furthermore, we addressed the documented verbosity bias [49, 50] by implementing a tiered length penalty. This mechanism enforces a strict brevity constraint: responses within a standard range (under 500 Chinese characters or 300 English words) receive no adjustment, while those exceeding the 500–800 (300–480) range require a one-level preference advantage to maintain parity. This ensures that final Elo ratings prioritize socio-pragmatic efficiency over token volume [51]. We also addressed the position bias and named participant bias by implementing cross-position evaluation and anonymiza-tion of all candidate responses. Detailed evaluation criteria and description of elo system please see Supplementary Information C.4 and D.

# A Definition of Emotional intelligence 

Emotional intelligence (EI) refers to the capacity to perceive, process, and regulate affective information in oneself and others to guide adaptive thought and behavior [40]. The most widely validated theoretical account is the four-branch ability model [41, 52], which organizes EI into perceiving emotions, using emotions to facilitate reasoning, understanding emotional dynamics, and managing emotions. This model was oper-ationalized through the MSCEIT [53], emphasizing performance-based assessment rather than self-report. Complementary competence-based models broaden the con-struct toward applied social skills such as empathy, adaptability, and relationship management [54]. Beyond this classical framework, subsequent theoretical and empirical develop-ments have enriched the construct of EI along two dimensions. The first concerns the cultural grounding of emotion itself. The theory of constructed emotion [29] argues that emotions are not biologically fixed categories but are actively constructed through culturally learned emotion concepts, and that both the repertoire of emotion cate-gories and the norms governing their expression are culturally shaped [55, 56]. This implies that emotional competence cannot be assessed in a culturally neutral manner, and that sensitivity to indirect, context-dependent modes of expression is itself a com-ponent of EI. The second concerns interactive competence. Research in counseling and psychotherapy identifies skills beyond perception and understanding that are essential for effective emotional engagement: facilitating emotional exploration by progressively deepening a client’s self-expression [57, 58], providing empathic attunement that con-veys genuine comprehension of the client’s inner experience [59], and recognizing psychological crises to ensure safety and guide appropriate intervention [22]. Integrating these perspectives, we conceptualize the emotional intelligence of a large language model as comprising three functional components: emotion percep-tion , the ability to identify affective states from both explicit and implicit linguistic cues; emotion cognition , the ability to reason about emotions, including predicting emotional responses from situational context, evaluating social appropriateness, and assessing psychological risk; and emotion interaction , the ability to generate affective responses that deepen exploration, convey empathy, regulate distress, and communi-cate naturally. These three components correspond to sequential yet dissociable stages of emotional processing, and are operationalized as the ten sub-dimensions of FACET. 37 B Related work 

B.1 Benchmarks for LLM Emotional Abilities 

As large language models continue to achieve breakthroughs across multiple domains, evaluating their emotional intelligence has become a crucial research direction. How-ever, the majority of existing benchmarks primarily concentrate on knowledge, reasoning, or generative abilities, offering limited assessment of emotional understand-ing and social interaction. In recent years, works such as EQ-Bench [12] have begun to explore the objective evaluation of emotional intelligence in large language models, seeking to quantify their comprehensive abilities in emotional understanding through tasks related to emotion perception, intensity judgment, and social context compre-hension. Building on the initial work, EQ-Bench 3 [60] shifts the focus from passive emotional understanding to the evaluation of active emotional intelligence through interactive, multi-turn role-play scenarios set in challenging contexts like relationship conflicts or workplace mediation. Another comprehensive framework is EmoBench [42], which evaluates machine EI across two primary dimensions grounded in psycho-logical theory: Emotional Understanding (EU) and Emotional Application (EA). The EU dimension uses hand-crafted scenarios to assess a model’s ability to identify com-plex emotions and their causes through reasoning. In parallel, the EA dimension tests the capacity to apply that understanding by requiring the model to select the most effective solution to various emotional dilemmas. The evaluation of emotional intelligence has also extended to multi-modal con-texts. A notable work in this area is EEmo-Bench [61], a benchmark designed to assess how Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) perceive image-evoked emotions. It employs a comprehensive emotional representation, combining a ranking of the top three evoked emotions with the Valence-Arousal-Dominance (VAD) model. The eval-uation is conducted across a four-task framework: Perception, Ranking, Description, and Assessment, which also includes comparative analysis of image pairs. Addressing the dynamics of ongoing conversations, EICAP-Bench [62] introduces a benchmark specifically for multi-turn emotional intelligence. The framework is built upon a hier-archical, psychologically-grounded taxonomy with four layers designed to assess LLM capabilities in evolving dialogues. These layers range from a Foundation layer for processing basic emotional signals and a Dimensional layer for tracking valence and arousal, to an Appraisal layer for causal inference and a Social layer for understanding cultural norms and mediating conflicts. Models are evaluated on their ability to track emotions across turns and select appropriate responses in a multiple-choice format.

Template Design. 

To ensure our benchmark is grounded in established psychological theory, the initial phase of our work involved the creation of standardized templates. Our expert col-laborators designed these templates for each dimension by referencing foundational studies in emotional intelligence. 

Seed Question Generation. 

Concurrently, we assembled an extensible catalogue of situational scenarios (see Appendix C.3) to steer item generation and enhance contextual diversity. Large-scale Generation. 

We used the rewritten items as seed questions (228 in total). Building on these seeds, and leveraging the scenario catalogue, we employed a few-shot prompting approach with the Claude-Sonnet-4 model to generate a large pool of candidate items. This phase yielded more than 800 candidate items, providing a rich material base for subsequent expert filtering and revision.

To facilitate a more rigorous selection of items, we developed a set of dimension-specific screening criteria (see C.4). For example, within the apparent emotion recognition dimension, key criteria included: 

• Clarity and naturalness of expression: retaining experiences that could plausibly occur in real life. 

• Item difficulty: preserving items that contain some confounding information to ensure a higher level of difficulty in emotional recognition. 

• Balance across emotional types, scenario types, and character roles 

• Option design and answer-key accuracy Guided by these screening criteria, we conducted a multi-round filtering process in which our experts systematically reviewed, refined, and—where necessary—revised the candidate items to ensure high quality and alignment with the benchmark’s objectives. The evaluation prompts are designed with a hierarchical structure to ensure that the LLM-as-a-judge aligns with professional psychological standards while maintaining high consistency. The prompt design incorporates several key components: 

• Expert-Authored Criteria : The comparison criteria and priority rankings (e.g., prioritizing open-ended questions over closed-ended ones) were meticulously developed by psychological experts to reflect clinical counseling principles. 

• Few-Shot Prompting and Chain-of-Thought Reasoning : To provide the model with concrete boundary cases, we employ a few-shot prompting strategy [64]. By including illustrative ”Exemplary” and ”Poor” response pairs, we anchor the evaluation scale and reduce subjective ambiguity. Furthermore, we incorpo-rate Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, requiring the judge model to provide a step-by-step analytical justification before reaching a final verdict. This approach has been shown to effectively mitigate inherent evaluative biases and improve the reliability of LLM-as-a-judge frameworks [21]. 

• Hierarchical Judgment Logic : Each prompt concludes with a structured reasoning process. The model is instructed to first analyze the psychological tech-niques used, then evaluate length appropriateness, and finally determine a winner based on an advantage-level system (e.g., ”+” to ”+++++”). 

• Length Penalty Mechanism : To mitigate the common ”verbosity bias” in large language models, a strict length penalty is integrated into the final judgment logic, favoring responses that remain concise and supportive for distressed users. 

C.5 Selection of Judge Model 

To establish a scalable evaluation framework, we adopt the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm, which has been empirically validated in recent studies. Prior work shows that strong LLM judges can achieve over 80% agreement with human experts in pairwise compar-isons, reaching a level comparable to human inter-rater agreement [21]. Furthermore, for open-ended and subjective generation tasks, LLM-based evaluators have been shown to achieve higher correlation with human judgments than traditional lexi-cal metrics, indicating their ability to better capture human-perceived quality [65]. The use of automated win rates derived from such comparisons is also supported by prior work, which demonstrates that LLM-based evaluations can approximate human preferences in a scalable manner [66]. Despite the general validity of this approach, the performance of an LLM judge can vary based on the specific domain and rubric complexity. Therefore, we imple-mented a systematic calibration process to select the primary judge for FACET. 42 We constructed a validation subset of 75 questions and obtained labels from three psychological experts, yielding a total of 405 expert-annotated instances. We then evaluated several leading models by calculating their inter-rater reliability with the expert annotations. During the systematic calibration, we conducted a comparative analysis of sev-eral state-of-the-art models, including GPT-5, DeepSeek-R1, and Gemini-2.5-Pro. The evaluation targeted five parallel dimensions defined in the FACET rubric: Emotion Deepening, Emotion Matching, Emotion Regulation, Empathetic Understanding, and Expression Naturalness. 

Robustness and High Performance Floor. 

Empirical results show that although multiple models align well with human experts, Gemini-2.5-Pro achieves the most balanced relative consistency across all dimensions. It maintains high Kappa values with a standard deviation of 0.0711 and a coefficient of variation (CV) of 0.1024, the lowest among all candidates. Its minimum Kappa (0.57) is also higher than other high-performing but less stable models. This suggests a lower risk of erratic scoring, even in highly ambiguous psychological conflict scenarios. 

Strategic Prioritization of Non-Formulaic Naturalness. 

To reduce the ”template bias” common in LLM judges—where models favor struc-tured but mechanical responses—we explicitly prioritized emotional naturalness. Gemini-2.5-Pro distinguished genuine emotional resonance from scripted empathy more effectively than other models. Using naturalness as a key criterion, the judge penalizes formulaic professional language, making final scores more consistent with the intuition of human counselors. 

Impartiality through Double-Blind ELO Scoring. 

To eliminate self-preference bias and position bias, we used a double-blind ELO scor-ing mechanism. Gemini-2.5-Pro evaluated response pairs without knowing the source model identities, and response order was randomized to avoid lead-position anchor-ing. This design, along with Gemini’s cross-dimensional consistency, supports its use as a proxy for human experts. Therefore, Gemini-2.5-Pro was selected as the primary judge for the full FACET evaluation.

Unlike the traditional Elo algorithm, TrueSkill characterizes model performance more precisely through probability distributions. The system models each model’s capability in a given dimension as a Gaussian distribution N (μ, σ 2), where the mean μ represents the estimated capability and the standard deviation σ denotes the uncertainty of this estimate. Following each pairwise matchup, the system dynamically adjusts the μ and 

σ of both models according to Bayesian update rules, ensuring that the evaluation process is statistically rigorous and convergent. 

Margin-aware Rating Update. 

To further explore the performance nuances between models, we incorporate a fine-grained ”Margin of Wins” mechanism, inspired by the EQ-Bench framework [68]. In the judge instructions C.4, the LLM judge is required not only to determine a winner but also to specify the degree of the lead based on expert criteria, using a five-level quantitative scale (ranging from ”+” to ”+++++”). During the aggregation phase, the system maps these symbolic advantage levels to non-linear margin coefficients, which are then converted into update step sizes. Consequently, a victory with an ”overwhelming advantage” contributes more significantly to the model’s μ value than a ”slight advantage.” 

Mitigation of Biases. 

To ensure the fairness of the evaluation results, we implemented experimental controls to address common cognitive biases in LLM judges: 

• Position Bias Mitigation : Research indicates that LLM judges often exhibit a tendency to favor specific display positions, such as a preference for the first option [21]. To address this, we perform cross-position evaluation [21] for every comparison request, conducting two independent assessments (”A vs B” and ”B vs A”). Results are only recorded when the two judgments are logically consistent or can be effectively aggregated. 

• Named Participant Bias Mitigation : If candidate responses include model names or other identifiers, they may trigger ”named participant bias” in the 44 judge model, leading to biased scoring. In our evaluation workflow, we implement complete anonymization of all candidate responses, replacing model names with neutral identifiers (e.g., Model A, Model B). This ensures that the judge model’s decisions are based solely on the psychological quality of the responses, excluding systematic biases introduced by brand effects or identity information. 

# E Limitations and Future Directions 

Although FACET provides a systematic framework to measure emotional intelli-gence in large language models, it has several limitations. These primarily include the reliance on text-only data, the difference between standardized test tasks and the spontaneous nature of real-life emotional interactions, and the complexity of cultural variations in emotions. Acknowledging these limits is necessary to interpret our results correctly. These constraints also suggest a path for moving from ”stochastic empa-thy”—which relies on statistical pattern matching—toward more context-aware and grounded emotional resonance. 

The Unimodal Proxy of Emotional Intelligence. 

A main limitation of this study is its reliance on text-only datasets, which are just a proxy for human emotional exchange. In real-life settings, emotional intelligence is closely tied to non-verbal signals like facial expressions and voice tones. While our focus on text establishes a necessary foundation for emotional reasoning, the good performance of models on text-centric tests may not stay the same in multimodal environments. Future research should combine verbal and non-verbal cues to fill this gap. 

Static Scenarios vs. Dynamic Dialogues. 

The FACET evaluation mostly focuses on static, single-turn scenarios. While this is effective for measuring ”atomic” emotional skills accurately, it does not fully cover the long-term process of real emotional support. Our framework currently has difficulty capturing how models manage emotional changes or build trust over a long time. Moving from these tested atomic units to continuous, long-term interaction models is an important next step. 

From Emotional Perception to Proactive Interaction. 

Our results reveal a significant gap between the models’ ability to perceive emotions and their capacity for effective interaction. While current models can identify affective states, they often struggle to translate this perception into contextually appropriate social responses. By finding this bottleneck, FACET acts as a tool to suggest adding psychological frameworks into model designs. Moving from simple pattern matching to a deep alignment of thought and emotion will require RLHF that understands not just ”what is safe,” but ”what fits” in specific social contexts. 45 Improving Hidden Emotion Recognition. 

Experiments show that Hidden Emotion Recognition is a common weak point, sug-gesting that models struggle to understand meaning beyond literal words. This is very clear in the complex logic of ”face culture.” While FACET points out this weakness, future work should use ”situated” dialogues to understand social power structures. By training on dialogues that keep their original social context, we can better understand the hidden feelings behind verbal compliance. 

Better Crisis Triage through Accuracy. 

We found a widespread conservative bias where models often overestimate how serious a crisis is. While this follows safety rules, it can push away users who are not in an emergency. We see this not as a safety failure, but as a need for better clinical accuracy. Future work should move from simple ”safe vs. unsafe” labels to expert-labeled, fine-grained benchmarks. This ensures that help matches the user’s actual risk level without breaking safety rules. 

More Natural Conversations without Templates. 

Data show that models often use standard emotional templates instead of adjusting to the specific feelings of a dialogue. While templates keep a baseline of safety, they can feel ”mechanical.” To fix this, future strategies should stop rewarding generic phrases like ”I understand how you feel” and instead focus on context-based empathy and natural styles. This change is necessary for models to move from just ”knowing” about emotions to actually acting naturally in different cultures.
