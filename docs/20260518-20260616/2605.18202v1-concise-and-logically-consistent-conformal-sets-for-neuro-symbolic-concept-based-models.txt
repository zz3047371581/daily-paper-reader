ABSTRACT 

Neuro-Symbolic Concept-based Models (NeSy-CBMs) are a family of architectures that integrate neural networks with symbolic reasoning for enhanced reliability in high-stakes applications. They work by first extracting high-level concepts from the input and then inferring a task label from these compatibly with given logical constraints. Yet, their label and concept predictions can be overconfident, making it difficult for stakeholders to gauge when the model’s decisions can be trusted. We address this issue by integrating ideas from Conformal Prediction (CP), a framework providing rigorous, distribution-free coverage guarantees. We formalize three desiderata – consistency, coverage, and conciseness – that any conformal method for NeSy-CBMs should satisfy, and show that existing approaches fall short of at least one. We then introduce COCOCO , a post-hoc framework that conformalizes concepts and labels jointly and reconciles them via a single deduction–abduction revision step . COCOCO satisfies all three desiderata, retains distribution-free coverage, is robust to imperfect knowledge and supports user-specified size budgets. Our experiments on 8 data sets highlight how COCOCO compares favorably against competitors and natural baselines in terms of performance and set size. 

1 Introduction 

Neuro-Symbolic AI (NeSy) architectures integrate neural networks with symbolic reasoning, and as such are prime candidates for designing reliable-by-construction AI systems [ 1, 2]. We focus on Neuro-Symbolic Concept-based Models ( NeSy-CBMs ), a general family of NeSy classifiers that distinguish between the neural and reasoning steps at the architectural level [ 3, 4 , 5]. Given a low-level input, a NeSy-CBM first distills it into a set of high-level concepts using a neural backbone, and then applies differentiable logic reasoning to infer a task prediction that complies to constraints we wish to hold. E.g., if tasked with computing the sum of two MNIST digits [ 6], it would first predict the corresponding digits (the concepts) and then apply the rule of addition (the constraint) [7]. Despite being geared for reliability, NeSy-CBMs can still be overconfident [ 8, 9], making their predictive uncertainty unreliable, see Fig. 1 (Left). This hinders their application to high-stakes tasks, where one often requires machine learning models to be aware of their own mistakes [ 10 ]. Moreover, even if a few NeSy approaches correct overconfidence in a principled manner (e.g., [ 8, 9 ]), their predicted probabilities can be difficult to grasp for practitioners and end-users [11]. We address these limitations with conformal prediction (CP), a general framework for constructing predictors that, rather than producing a point-wise probability estimate, output a set of plausible alternatives with finite-sample and distribution-free coverage guarantees [ 12 ]. Neuro-Symbolic AI . NeSy approaches differ wildly in terms of aim and architecture [ 1, 2]. We focus on Neuro-Symbolic Concept-based Models ( NeSy-CBMs for short), a family of classifiers designed for reliability [ 3, 4, 5 ]. All NeSy-CBMs extract intermediate, high-level concepts 1 from the input and infer predictions by 

reasoning over the concepts compatibly with user-specified prior knowledge. The latter is encoded as a logic formula K. A NeSy-CBM [ 22 , 23 , 5] maps inputs x ∈ X to k concepts c = ( c1, . . . , c k) ∈ C and then to labels y ∈ Y using two components. The concept extractor f : X → ∆C is a neural network mapping the input x ∈ X to a distribution over concepts pθ (C | x). The inference layer g : C → ∆Y maps concept vectors to a distribution over labels p(Y | c, K) according to given and fixed prior knowledge K. With slight abuse of notation, we write (g ◦ f ) to indicate the conditional distribution p(Y | x; K) obtained by passing the concept probabilities output by f to the inference layer g. In practice, how these are combined depends on the specific NeSy-CBM [5]; we describe the two we use experimentally below, after a running example.

3 Building Blocks of Conformal NeSy-CBMs 

NeSy-CBMs are geared for reliability in high-stakes applications [ 5, 38 , 39 ], where it is essential that predictions satisfy domain-specific constraints K [ 7, 40 , 41 , 42 ]. Yet, their predictive uncertainty is both unreliable and difficult to understand. In fact, they are often miscalibrated [ 8 , 9, 10 ], meaning stakeholders cannot use their uncertainty to assess whether to trust their predictions [ 8, 9 ]. Moreover, predictive uncertainty is difficult for users to understand in the first place [ 11 ]. Both issues are problematic, especially for NeSy-CBMs affected by reasoning shortcuts, in which case users ought to be made aware that the learned concepts are not human aligned, yet these are often predicted wrong with confidence [5, 23]. This calls for a different strategy that can simultaneously handle uncertainty while providing a user-friendly interface for reliability assessment. This is where CP comes into play, as it offers a principled, distribution-free remedy that does not involve communicating probabilities to users. We outline the benefits we wish to obtain from such a combination by formalizing three desiderata that any NeSy conformal approach ought to satisfy: 

D1 Consistency : predicted concepts and labels must be logically consistent with the knowledge K.

D3 Conciseness : prediction sets should be as small as possible, retaining only relevant alternatives – large sets are uninterpretable and operationally useless [32]. In Section 4, we will introduce COCOCO , a novel NeSy CP strategies that does complies with D1 –D3 . Yet, integrating CP into NeSy-CBMs without compromising any desideratum is non-trivial. In fact, as we will show in Section 4.1, any “ one-sided ” CP strategy that independently targets either the concept extractor or the inference layer is insufficient to satisfy all desiderata. 

Inference layer operators . Before proceeding, we introduce two operators that all methods we will discuss build on, together with their coverage properties. We denote with g† the argmax of the inference layer g and its preimage as g−† , that is: 

g†(c) := argmax y∈Y g(c) ⊆ Y , g−† (y) := {c ∈ C : y ∈ g†(c)}. (3) Intuitively, g† deduces a (subset of) label(s) from given concepts, while g−† abduces a (subset of) concepts from given label(s). Two properties of g† play a central role in our coverage guarantees: 

Definition 3.1 (Deductive & Abductive Soundness) . Let p∗(x, c, y) be the joint data distribution. A NeSy-CBM (f, g ) satisfies deductive soundness if, for every (c∗, y∗) ∈ supp( p∗(x, c, y)) , g†(c∗) = y∗. It satisfies 

abductive soundness if, for every (c∗, y∗) ∈ supp( p∗(x, c, y)) , c∗ ∈ g−† (y∗).Deductive and abductive soundness depend on both the prior knowledge K and on how the inference layer 

g implements it. In particular, they hold when: ( i) the knowledge is correct and complete , meaning that it allows the ground-truth labels and concepts to be retrieved from one another without errors (as in MN-Add , see Example 1), and ( ii ) the inference layer g preserves this property . The latter holds for both DPL and LTN 3 – and in general for most NeSy-CBMs [ 40 , 43 , 41 ]. However, these properties may not hold when the knowledge is incomplete or erroneous, as for some of the datasets used in our experiments (Section 6), or when the inference layer is learned from data [44, 45, 46, 47].

4 Consistency, Conformality and Conciseness 

We begin by introducing COCOCO (COnformality, COnsistency, COnciseness), a post-hoc framework for turning any NeSy-CBM into a conformal predictor satisfying all three desiderata D1 –D3 . The idea is as follows: starting from a label set Υα(x) and a concept set Γβ (x), we make them mutually consistent by intersecting the label set (resp. concept set) with g† applied to the concept set (resp. g−† to the label set). We show that this requires only one revision step to converge.

User-specified size budgets . Standard CP fixes α, β a priori and yields the corresponding set sizes. In practice, specifically in decision-support systems [ 31 , 34 , 32 ], it is often more useful to fix a size budget (“at most 5

candidate concepts”) and report the corresponding coverage, thereby avoiding overwhelming practitioners with a range of choices. As shown by Gauthier et al. [19] , this can be achieved by replacing standard CP with 

conformal e-predictions [ 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 ], which use e-values [13 , 14 , 15 , 16 ] in place of quantiles. E-values bring two benefits to COCOCO . First, we can provably build a conformal set over concepts while avoiding Bonferroni’s correction (Corollary C.4), meaning that the resulting concept-set guarantee does not degrade with the number of concepts k (Proposition C.9). Second, e-values allow data-dependent miscoverage levels ˜α, ˜β to be chosen after observing calibration scores, in particular to satisfy user-specified size budgets while retaining a coverage guarantee in expectation [ 16 , 17 ]. We denote this variant COCOCO ∗, and prove its joint-coverage guarantee in Section C.10. Details about e-values and COCOCO ∗ are left to Section D.3 due to space constraints. 

Runtime . A key advantage of COCOCO is that the joint revision operator reaches a fixed point in a single step (Proposition 4.1), so the additional overhead amounts to computing the abductive and deductive images once, i.e., Γab (x) = g−† (Υ α(x)) and Υde (x) = g†(Γ β (x)) . The main computational bottleneck lies in constructing the concept set Γ×(x), which scales exponentially in the number of concepts k due to the cross-product structure. This issue is mitigated when full concept annotations are available or if COCOCO ∗ is employed due to e-values , in which cases Γβ (x) can be obtained carrying out any set product. In practice, we find that the runtime of COCOCO does not exceed 1.5× that of standard DPL and LTN , which is acceptable in high-stakes settings where reliability is crucial. A detailed runtime analysis is deferred to Section F.4 due to space constraints.

5 Related Work 

Conformal Prediction . CP produces prediction sets containing the ground-truth value with a pre-specified coverage [ 12 ]. Core theoretical guarantees are established by Papadopoulos et al. [36] . Extensions cover regression [ 54 , 55 ]; conditional guarantees [ 56 , 57 ]; noisy ground truth [ 58 , 59 ], covariate shifts [ 60 ] and size control [ 19 , 20 ]. We refer the reader to Angelopoulos and Bates [37] for a broader survey on conformal prediction. 

Conformal prediction for NeSy . The two closest works are Ledaguenel et al. [53] , who apply CP only at the label level (matching our TO baseline and inheriting its lack of concept-side coverage), and Ramalingam et al. [49] , who propose three strategies for symbolic programs over bounding-boxes that align with our CDe , TO ,and RPB baselines. Both are subsumed by our framework. COCOCO goes further by also revising the concept set via g−† (Υ α(x)) , which is what guarantees concept-side consistency and drives the empirical concept-set reductions reported in Section 6. Other lines of work [ 61 , 62 ] use CP for LLM-generated programs and are orthogonal to ours. 

NeSy-CBMs and Reasoning Shortcuts . NeSy-CBMs constitute a broad family of architectures that combine neural concept extractors with symbolic inference layers [ 1 , 22 ], encompassing models based on probabilistic logic [ 7 , 63 , 64 , 40 , 52 , 65 ], fuzzy logic [ 28 , 27 ], and algebraic constraints [ 41 , 43 ]. A known failure mode of these models is that of reasoning shortcuts [ 23 ], whereby the concept extractor learns incorrect concepts that allow to predict correct labels in training [ 5]. This phenomenon is mainly tackled by resorting to additional supervision [23, 66] and uncertainty estimation [8, 9].

COCOCO attains concept consistency 1.00 across all four datasets, whereas RPB ranges from 0.12 (MN-EO )to 0.94 (RIV10 ). 7 Conclusion 

We introduced COCOCO , a post-hoc conformal framework for NeSy-CBMs that jointly conformalizes concept and label predictions and reconciles them via a single deduction–abduction revision step. This step provably yields the largest logically consistent revision of the two sets and reaches a fixed point in one pass (Proposi-tion 4.1). The framework retains distribution-free coverage that degrades controllably under imperfect knowl-edge, and – combined with e-values – supports user-specified size budgets. To our knowledge, COCOCO is the first method that exploits symbolic knowledge to simultaneously shrink conformal prediction sets and enforce label–concept consistency while retaining distribution-free coverage. Empirically, COCOCO consistently attains target coverage while reducing concept-set size on eight benchmarks. 

Limitations and Future Work . Abduction and deduction require enumerating g†(Γ β (x)) and g−† (Υ α(x)) ,while Γ×(x) requires to compute the product over a possibly large number of concepts. This becomes a bottleneck for very large concept spaces (e.g., BDD-OIA [ 71 , 70 ]). Similarly, regarding inference time, the inference layer g is called multiple times; while knowledge-compiled circuits [25, 72, 26, 73, 74] (as in DPL )make this tractable in practice. The product-set Γ×(x) – if the cross-product construction is used – assume conditional independence (Proposition C.9), inherited from standard NeSyCBMs [ 30 , 9]. Importantly, the 

COCOCO revision step itself does not require independence: it operates on any concept set satisfying Eq. (6). Plugging in expressive concept distributions [ 30 , 9] is a direct extension. As future work, we plan to extend 

COCOCO to a broader range of NeSy-CBMs architectures, leveraging, once mature, recent initiatives that aim to make NeSy-AI more accessible and easier to adopt for practitioners, such as DeepLog [ 74 ] and ULLER [ 75 ], and to architectures that learn the knowledge from data [ 44 , 45 , 46 , 47 ], where soundness can no longer be assumed a priori and must be estimated, affecting the bounds of Proposition 4.2.

Broader Impact 

By coupling distribution-free coverage with logical consistency, COCOCO makes NeSy-CBMs suitable for decision-support systems [ 31 , 32 , 34 ], particularly in high-stakes settings such as CHX where practitioners consume both intermediate concepts and final labels. We foresee no negative societal impact specific to 

COCOCO beyond those shared by any conformal method. 

A Additional Related Work 

Uncertainty quantification in NeSy-CBMs . NeSy-CBMs, despite their architectural bias toward reliability, are not immune to overconfident predictions [ 8]. Existing approaches fall into two categories. The first modifies the concept distribution to be more expressive [ 30 ], e.g., via deep ensembles [ 8] or diffusion-based parametrizations [ 9]. The second enforces uncertainty-related properties during training via constraint-based objectives [ 76 ]. Our approach is complementary and post-hoc: it operates on top of an already-trained NeSy-CBM and provides distribution-free coverage on top of any of these methods. 

B Extended One-Sided Strategies and Prior Work 

COCOCO combines two design choices: (i) constructing both a label set Υα(x) and a concept set Γβ (x), and (ii) revising each via the logical image of the other. A natural question is whether either choice can be relaxed without sacrificing the desiderata. In this section we systematically ablate COCOCO along these two axes, showing that each one-sided strategy fails at least one desideratum. The ablations also subsume prior work: Ledaguenel et al. D.5.1 MN-Add MN-Add , originally introduced in Manhaeve et al. [7] , is the canonical benchmark in Neuro-Symbolic AI. Each input consists of two MNIST [ 6] digit images, and the target label is their arithmetic sum. Formally, each sample is a pair of images (x1, x2) representing MNIST digits (c1, c2), each taking values in [0 , 9] , and the task is to predict: 

y = c1 + c2, (122) In contrast to MN-EO and MN-H , MN-Add contains all possible digit combinations ( 54 , 000 train examples, 6, 000 

validation examples and 10 , 000 test examples) and does not induce structural ambiguity in the digit-to-value mapping. The underlying combinatorial structure uniquely identifies each digit, and no deterministic reasoning shortcuts arise. Consequently, MN-Add erves as a shortcut-free reference benchmark for reasoning [22]. 

Reasoning Shortcuts . MN-Add is provably reasoning shortcuts free. For further information refer to Bortolotti et al. [70].

F.2 Product vs. Average e-value Aggregation: when Assumptions do not hold

F.3 The Full Spectrum of NeSy Conformal Prediction 

In the main text we compared COCOCO only against RPB , the strongest competitor. Here we report the full ablation against the four natural conformalization strategies introduced in Section B: TO , TAb , CO , and CDe .The goal is threefold: (i) confirm that the two-sided joint revision of COCOCO is genuinely necessary and that no simpler one-sided strategy achieves the same trade-off across D1 –D3 ; (ii) quantify the failure modes of each baseline across the eight data sets; and (iii) ground the design choices of COCOCO empirically. The full numerical results are Tables 7 to 29; we summarise the qualitative results below. Throughout, the nominal coverage targets are 1 − α = 1 − β = 0 .9.

TO offers conformalized labels but uncalibrated, inconsistent concepts . TO applies CP to the label distribution pθ (Y | x, K) directly. As predicted by Eq. (4), label coverage hits the nominal 1 − α = 0 .9

target on every data set (e.g., 0.90 on MN-Add , 0.89 on CHX with DPL , 0.90 on RIV10 , CEB , and CIF10 ). On the concept side, however, TO returns the single argmax concept (size 1), so concept coverage collapses to the base concept accuracy of the underlying NeSy-CBM: as low as 0.18 on CEB (DPL , supervised), 0.00 on 

CEB unsupervised ( DPL and LTN ), 0.00 and 0.13 on MN-EO (LTN and DPL , respectively) and 0.03 on MN-SP 

(DPL and LTN ). Worse, label consistency drops well below 1 whenever the conformal label set is non-trivial: e.g., 0.20 on CHX (LTN ), 0.20 -0.42 on CEB , and 0.62 on CHX (DPL , supervised). TO thus violates both D1 

(consistency) and D2 (concept-side coverage). 

CO achieves conformalized concepts but uncalibrated labels . CO applies CP at the concept level using the product construction of Eq. (5) with Bonferroni’s correction (Corollary C.4). Concept coverage matches the target on most data sets ( 0.87 -1.00 ). However, CO returns the single argmax label, so the label side inherits the base label accuracy. Label consistency drops: 0.08 -0.40 on MN-EO , MN-H , CEB , and CHX . Bonferroni’s correction (Corollary C.4) inflates the concept set as the number of concepts grows: 75 .06 on CEB (DPL ,45 Concise and Logically Consistent Conformal Sets for Neuro-Symbolic Concept-Based Models A P REPRINT 

supervised), 57 .60 on CIF10 (DPL , unsupervised), 100 .00 , the entire product space, on MN-SP (LTN ) and 

MN-EO (LTN and DPL ). CO thus partially satisfies D2 on the concept side but fails both D1 and D3 .

TAb achieves consistency and coverage but not conciseness . TAb constructs a concept set via abduction, 

Γab (x) = g−† (Υ α(x)) . This recovers both coverage and consistency at the concept level by construction: on every data set, TAb attains high concept coverage ≥ 0.89 and 1.00 consistency by construction. The caveat is concept-size. Because abduction indiscriminately includes every concept vector consistent with some label in Υα(x), the resulting set is an order of magnitude larger than any competitor: 504 .38 and 742 .55 on CEB 

for DPL , supervised and unsupervised setting respectively, 1024 for LTN instead (the entire concept space), 

171 .66 and 288 .11 on CIF10 for DPL and LTN unsupervised, 2.20 -50 .00 on the MNIST variants. TAb therefore satisfies D1 –D2 on the concept side but fails D3 dramatically. 

CDe achieves consistency but inflated label sets . CDe constructs a label set by deducing g†(Γ β (x)) from the concept set. It achieves both label and concept coverage near the nominal level and 1.00 consistency by construction (Proposition 3.3). The failure mode is on the label side: the deduced label set can be substantially larger than TO . On CEB (DPL , supervised), as an example, CDe produces label sets of size 3.71 vs. 2.41 of TO 

and on MN-H (DPL ) 9.00 vs. 0.89 . And the same trend could be observed in every tested datasets. The issue is that every element of Γ×(x) – even low-probability ones – contributes a label to Υde (x). CDe is therefore a reasonable baseline when label size is not a binding constraint, but it inflates the label set failing D3 on the labels. 

RPB closes label-side consistency but leaves the concept set inconsistent . RPB [ 49 ] revises the label set as Υrev (x) = Υ α(x) ∩ g†(Γ×(x)) , but leaves Γ×(x) untouched. It therefore produces identical label sets to COCOCO by construction, but underperforms on the concept side: concept consistency is typically 0.50 -

0.94 across the eight data sets (vs. 1.00 for COCOCO ), and the concept set is at least as large as COCOCO ’s in every case where the two differ. Representative gaps include CEB (DPL , supervised), 0.78 → 1.00 consistency, 

75 .06 → 53 .49 size, CHX (DPL, supervised) 0.55 → 1.00 , 5.30 → 2.90 , MN-EO (DPL ) 0.12 → 1.00 ,

100 .00 → 12 .13 , MN-EO (LTN ) 0.08 → 1.00 , 100 .00 → 8.05 and MN-H (DPL ) 0.09 → 1.00 , 25 .00 → 2.20 .The takeaway is that revising only the label side leaves the concept side inconsistent with the deduced labels; the symmetric revision in COCOCO is what closes this gap and delivers D1 on both sides while preserving D2 .Summary. Each one-sided baseline achieves one desideratum at the cost of another: TO ensures label coverage but sacrifices concept coverage and consistency; CO ensures concept coverage but sacrifices consistency and concept conciseness; TAb ensures concept coverage and consistency but sacrifices concept conciseness; CDe 

ensures consistency but sacrifices label conciseness; RPB ensures label consistency but leaves the concept set inflated and inconsistent. COCOCO is the only method to revise both sides symmetrically in a single deduction– abduction step (Proposition C.7) and to attain D1 , D2 , and D3 simultaneously across all eight data sets and both NeSy backbones. 

F.4 Time Overhead Analysis 

A key point of COCOCO is that the joint revision operator reaches a fixed point in one step (Proposition 4.1), making the additional cost a single computation of the abductive and deductive images, i.e., Γab (x) = 

g−† (Υ α(x)) and Υde (x) = g†(Γ β (x)) .In practice, as shown by Section F.4 and Fig. 10, COCOCO is comparable in runtime to all other ablations. While it is naturally slower than TO and RPB since it requires additional revision steps, Proposition C.7 ensures it remains tractable thanks to the fixed-point theorem (Proposition C.7). Looking at the aggregate results in Section F.4, COCOCO exhibits an overhead of approximately 160% relative to DPL and LTN when considered individually in the unsupervised setting ( 100% horizontal line), and around 

120% in the supervised setting. This is comparable to RPB (140% and 110% , respectively) and to CDe 

(130 − 150% unsupervised), and only moderately higher than TO , which is the closest to the baseline. Looking at individual datasets in Fig. 10, the overhead of COCOCO is dataset-dependent, as it depends on the number of concepts and labels produced by the conformal method, but remains consistently bounded, except for CEB , where the number of concept combinations is 4× higher than the baseline due to the large number of generated concepts and labels. Notably, in CHX – our operational dataset – and RIV10 , the overhead is almost non-existent, demonstrating that in practical applications the overhead is negligible. In fact, COCOCO is well-suited to settings where a human is in the loop and the number of concepts and produced labels is close to one, precisely the scenario in which COCOCO excels. 46 Concise and Logically Consistent Conformal Sets for Neuro-Symbolic Concept-Based Models A P REPRINT 

Furthermore, when employing COCOCO with e-values ( COCOCO ∗), as well as in other ablations, the computa-tional overhead is approximately 200 − 250% in the unsupervised setting and close to 150% in the supervised setting. This is worse than COCOCO even with the speed-up arising from avoiding the Bonferroni correction (Proposition C.9), as it eliminates the need to enumerate the full product set (Eq. (5)). COCOCO ∗, used in a p-value-like fashion, produces consistently larger concept and label sets, except in CHX and RIV10 . Nevertheless, unlike all other approaches, COCOCO ∗ can be fixed in size and thus in computational overhead. In fact, with appropriate choices of ˆβ and ˆα – namely those that keep the set size below a given threshold (as shown in Sec-tion 6) – we can cap the computational cost, since we know in advance the size of the instances on which the inference layer g will be applied.
